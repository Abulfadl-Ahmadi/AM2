## 1. Angular Momentum of a System

**Original Notes Transcription:**
> In an inertial coordinate system (I.F.):
> $\vec{L} = \sum_\alpha \vec{L}_\alpha = \sum_\alpha \vec{r}_\alpha \times \vec{P}_\alpha$
> Using the center of mass decomposition:
> (I) $\vec{r}_\alpha = \vec{R}_{CM} + \vec{r}'_\alpha$
> (II) $\vec{P}_\alpha = m_\alpha (\dot{\vec{R}}_{CM} + \dot{\vec{r}}'_\alpha)$
> $\rightarrow \vec{L} = \sum_\alpha m_\alpha (\vec{R}_{CM} + \vec{r}'_\alpha) \times (\dot{\vec{R}}_{CM} + \dot{\vec{r}}'_\alpha)$
> Expanding the cross product gives 4 terms:
> $= \underbrace{\sum_\alpha m_\alpha \vec{R}_{CM} \times \dot{\vec{R}}_{CM}}_{(I)} + \underbrace{\sum_\alpha m_\alpha \vec{R}_{CM} \times \dot{\vec{r}}'_\alpha}_{(II)} + \underbrace{\sum_\alpha m_\alpha \vec{r}'_\alpha \times \dot{\vec{R}}_{CM}}_{(III)} + \underbrace{\sum_\alpha m_\alpha \vec{r}'_\alpha \times \dot{\vec{r}}'_\alpha}_{(IV)}$

> **Elaboration:**
> To calculate the total angular momentum $\vec{L}$ of a system of particles, we sum the individual angular momenta of all particles relative to an inertial origin. A powerful technique is to decompose each particle's position vector $\vec{r}_\alpha$ into two parts: the position of the center of mass $\vec{R}_{CM}$, and the particle's position relative to the CM, $\vec{r}'_\alpha$. Substituting this into the angular momentum definition yields four terms.

**Original Notes Transcription:**
> Let's analyze the terms:
> Term (I): $M \vec{R}_{CM} \times \vec{V}_{CM}$ (Angular momentum of the center of mass)
> Term (II): $\vec{R}_{CM} \times \frac{d}{dt}\left(\sum_\alpha m_\alpha \vec{r}'_\alpha\right) = \vec{R}_{CM} \times \frac{d}{dt}(0) = 0$
> Term (III): $\left(\sum_\alpha m_\alpha \vec{r}'_\alpha\right) \times \vec{V}_{CM} = 0 \times \vec{V}_{CM} = 0$
> Term (IV): $\sum_\alpha \vec{r}'_\alpha \times \vec{p}'_\alpha$ (Angular momentum of the particles in the CM frame)
> 
> $\Rightarrow \vec{L} = \vec{R}_{CM} \times \vec{P}_{CM} + \sum_\alpha \vec{r}'_\alpha \times \vec{p}'_\alpha$

> **Elaboration:**
> The definition of the center of mass implies that the mass-weighted sum of positions relative to the CM is always zero ($\sum m_\alpha \vec{r}'_\alpha = 0$). Because of this, the cross terms (II and III) perfectly cancel out. 
> The final elegant result shows that the total angular momentum is simply the sum of two independent parts:
> 1. The **Orbital Angular Momentum** ($\vec{R}_{CM} \times \vec{P}_{CM}$): Treating the entire system as a single point mass at the CM.
> 2. The **Spin Angular Momentum** ($\sum \vec{r}'_\alpha \times \vec{p}'_\alpha$): The internal rotation of the system's particles around its own CM.

## 2. Torque and Total Torque

**Original Notes Transcription:**
> $\dot{\vec{P}} = \sum_\alpha \vec{f}_\alpha = \sum_\alpha \vec{f}_\alpha^{int} + \sum_\alpha \vec{f}_\alpha^{ext}$
> *Confession: Internal forces are not necessarily along the line connecting the particles.*
> Definition of Torque: $\vec{\tau}_\alpha = \vec{r}_\alpha \times \vec{f}_\alpha = \vec{r}_\alpha \times (\vec{f}_\alpha^{int} + \vec{f}_\alpha^{ext})$
> Total Torque: $\vec{\tau} = \sum_\alpha \vec{\tau}_\alpha = \underbrace{\sum_\alpha \vec{r}_\alpha \times \vec{f}_\alpha^{int}}_{(I)} + \underbrace{\sum_\alpha \vec{r}_\alpha \times \vec{f}_\alpha^{ext}}_{(II)}$

> **Elaboration:**
> To find how angular momentum changes, we must look at the total torque ($\vec{\tau}$). Just like forces, torques are generated by both internal and external forces. However, an important caveat is made here: while Newton's third law says internal forces are equal and opposite, it doesn't strictly guarantee they point along the line connecting the two interacting particles. This is known as the "Weak Form" of Newton's 3rd Law.

### Internal Torques Cancellation

**Original Notes Transcription:**
> Term (I): $\sum_\alpha \vec{r}_\alpha \times \vec{f}_\alpha^{int}$
> Internal force on $\alpha$: $\vec{f}_\alpha^{int} = \sum_{\beta \neq \alpha} \vec{f}_{\alpha\beta}$
> $\sum_\alpha \vec{r}_\alpha \times \vec{f}_\alpha^{int} = \sum_{\alpha} \sum_{\beta < \alpha} \vec{r}_\alpha \times \vec{f}_{\alpha\beta} + \sum_{\alpha} \sum_{\beta > \alpha} \vec{r}_\alpha \times \vec{f}_{\alpha\beta}$
> Swapping indices $\alpha \leftrightarrow \beta$ in the second sum and using $\vec{f}_{\beta\alpha} = -\vec{f}_{\alpha\beta}$:
> $= \sum_{\beta < \alpha} (\vec{r}_\alpha - \vec{r}_\beta) \times \vec{f}_{\alpha\beta}$
> 
> *If we assume Newton's third law in its strong form holds, then $\vec{f}_{\alpha\beta}$ is parallel to $(\vec{r}_\alpha - \vec{r}_\beta)$, so the above sum becomes zero!*
> $\rightarrow \sum_\alpha \vec{r}_\alpha \times \vec{f}_\alpha^{int} = 0 \Rightarrow \vec{\tau} = \vec{\tau}_{ext}$

> **Elaboration:**
> To prove that internal forces do not create a net torque on the system, we pair up the internal interactions between all particles $\alpha$ and $\beta$. This results in a sum of terms involving $(\vec{r}_\alpha - \vec{r}_\beta) \times \vec{f}_{\alpha\beta}$. 
> **The Strong Form of Newton's 3rd Law** states that internal forces not only are equal and opposite, but they also act exactly along the line connecting the two particles. If this holds, the cross product is zero (since the vectors are parallel), and all internal torques cancel out. Thus, only external torques can affect the system.

## 3. Rate of Change of Angular Momentum

**Original Notes Transcription:**
> $\vec{L} = \sum_\alpha \vec{r}_\alpha \times \vec{P}_\alpha$
> $\dot{\vec{L}} = \sum_\alpha \dot{\vec{r}}_\alpha \times \vec{P}_\alpha + \sum_\alpha \vec{r}_\alpha \times \dot{\vec{P}}_\alpha$
> $= 0 + \sum_\alpha \vec{r}_\alpha \times \vec{f}_\alpha = \vec{\tau}_{ext}$
> $\Rightarrow \frac{d\vec{L}}{dt} = \vec{\tau}_{ext}$ (In an inertial coordinate system)

> **Elaboration:**
> By taking the time derivative of the total angular momentum, the first term vanishes because velocity is parallel to momentum ($\dot{\vec{r}} \times m\dot{\vec{r}} = 0$). The second term is simply the definition of torque. Therefore, the rate of change of the total angular momentum is exactly equal to the net external torque applied to the system, analogous to $\vec{F}_{ext} = d\vec{P}/dt$.

### Non-Inertial Frames and Fictitious Gravity

**Original Notes Transcription:**
> *Question: Is there any other coordinate system in which a similar relation holds?*
> **Note I Example:** Total torque due to weight force.
> $\vec{\tau}_{ext} = \sum_\alpha m_\alpha \vec{r}_\alpha \times \vec{g} = \left(\frac{1}{M} \sum m_\alpha \vec{r}_\alpha\right) \times M\vec{g} = \vec{R}_{CM} \times M\vec{g}$
> 
> **Note II Question:** Car-frame (accelerating with $\vec{A}$).
> *We must apply a compensatory force $-m_\alpha \vec{A}$ to each particle in the non-inertial frame.*
> *It's like a fictitious gravity with field strength $-\vec{A}$.*
> Compensatory torque: $\vec{\tau}_{\vec{A}} = \vec{R}_{CM} \times (-M\vec{A})$
> 
> **Note III:**
> *So by choosing the CM as the origin of coordinates (in a non-inertial frame), we can again easily use the relation $\vec{\tau} = \frac{d\vec{L}}{dt}$.*
> $\triangle$ *WARNING: This statement is only true for non-rotating frames.*

> **Elaboration:**
> Normally, $\vec{\tau}_{ext} = \dot{\vec{L}}$ only holds in an inertial (non-accelerating) reference frame. However, an accelerating frame (like a moving car) introduces fictitious forces ($-m\vec{A}$) on every particle. 
> Because this fictitious force is proportional to mass (just like real gravity), we can calculate its net torque exactly like we did for gravity in Note I: the net fictitious torque acts entirely at the Center of Mass ($\vec{R}_{CM} \times -M\vec{A}$).
> If we place our coordinate origin exactly AT the Center of Mass, then $\vec{R}_{CM} = 0$, meaning the fictitious torque vanishes! Thus, even in an accelerating reference frame, the equation $\vec{\tau}_{ext} = \dot{\vec{L}}$ still perfectly holds true **as long as the origin is the Center of Mass** and the frame is not rotating.

## 4. Angular Momentum and Rotation

**Original Notes Transcription:**
> **Note IV:**
> The derived relation might not be very straightforward for calculation.
> $\vec{L} = \sum_\alpha \vec{r}_\alpha \times \vec{P}_\alpha$
> For a body rotating with angular velocity $\vec{\omega} = \omega \hat{n}$:
> Velocity of particle $\alpha$: $\vec{v}_\alpha = \vec{\omega} \times \vec{r}_\alpha \Rightarrow \vec{P}_\alpha = m_\alpha (\vec{\omega} \times \vec{r}_\alpha)$
> $\Rightarrow \vec{L} = \sum_\alpha m_\alpha \vec{r}_\alpha \times (\vec{\omega} \times \vec{r}_\alpha)$

> **Elaboration:**
> While the general formula for $\vec{L}$ is $\sum \vec{r} \times \vec{p}$, for rigid bodies rotating around an axis, it is much more practical to express momentum in terms of the angular velocity vector $\vec{\omega}$. Substituting $\vec{v} = \vec{\omega} \times \vec{r}$ into the angular momentum equation leads to the definition of the Moment of Inertia tensor, mapping angular velocity directly to angular momentum. *(To be continued in the next lecture).*
