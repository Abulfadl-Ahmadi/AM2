### Original Transcribed Notes

We now direct our attention to a rigid body composed of $n$ particles of masses $m_\alpha$, $\alpha = 1, 2, 3, \dots, n$. If the body rotates with an instantaneous angular velocity $\boldsymbol{\omega}$ about some point fixed with respect to the body coordinate system, and if this point moves with an instantaneous linear velocity $\mathbf{V}$ with respect to the fixed coordinate system, then the instantaneous velocity of the $\alpha$th particle in the fixed system can be obtained by using Equation 10.17. But we are now considering a rigid body, so the relative velocity with respect to the body system vanishes:

$$(\mathbf{v}_\alpha)_{\text{body}} = 0$$

Therefore,

$$\mathbf{v}_\alpha = \mathbf{V} + \boldsymbol{\omega} \times \mathbf{r}_\alpha$$
*(11.4)*

where the subscript $f$, denoting the fixed coordinate system, has been deleted from the velocity $\mathbf{v}_\alpha$, it now being understood that all velocities are measured in the fixed system.

Because the kinetic energy of the $\alpha$th particle is given by

$$T_\alpha = \frac{1}{2} m_\alpha v_\alpha^2$$

we have, for the total kinetic energy,

$$T = \frac{1}{2} \sum_{\alpha} m_\alpha \left( \mathbf{V} + \boldsymbol{\omega} \times \mathbf{r}_\alpha \right)^2$$
$$T = \frac{1}{2} \sum_{\alpha} m_\alpha V^2 + \sum_{\alpha} m_\alpha \mathbf{V} \cdot \left( \boldsymbol{\omega} \times \mathbf{r}_\alpha \right) + \frac{1}{2} \sum_{\alpha} m_\alpha \left( \boldsymbol{\omega} \times \mathbf{r}_\alpha \right)^2$$
*(11.5)*

This is a general expression for the kinetic energy and is valid for any choice of the origin from which the vectors $\mathbf{r}_\alpha$ are measured. But if we make the origin of the body coordinate system coincide with the center of mass of the object, a considerable simplification results. First, we note that in the second term on the right-hand side of this equation, neither $\mathbf{V}$ nor $\boldsymbol{\omega}$ is characteristic of the $\alpha$th particle, and therefore, these quantities may be taken outside the summation:

$$\sum_{\alpha} m_\alpha \mathbf{V} \cdot \left( \boldsymbol{\omega} \times \mathbf{r}_\alpha \right) = \mathbf{V} \cdot \left[ \boldsymbol{\omega} \times \left( \sum_{\alpha} m_\alpha \mathbf{r}_\alpha \right) \right]$$

But now the term

$$\sum_{\alpha} m_\alpha \mathbf{r}_\alpha = M \mathbf{R}$$

is the center-of-mass vector (see Equation 9.3), which vanishes in the body system because the vectors $\mathbf{r}_\alpha$ are measured from the center of mass ($\mathbf{R} = 0$). The kinetic energy can then be written as

$$T = T_{\text{trans}} + T_{\text{rot}}$$

where

$$T_{\text{trans}} = \frac{1}{2} M V^2$$
*(11.6a)*

$$T_{\text{rot}} = \frac{1}{2} \sum_{\alpha} m_\alpha \left( \boldsymbol{\omega} \times \mathbf{r}_\alpha \right)^2$$
*(11.6b)*

$T_{\text{trans}}$ and $T_{\text{rot}}$ designate the translational and rotational kinetic energies, respectively. Thus, the kinetic energy separates into two independent parts.

The rotational kinetic energy term can be evaluated by noting that

$$(\mathbf{A} \times \mathbf{B})^2 = (\mathbf{A} \times \mathbf{B}) \cdot (\mathbf{A} \times \mathbf{B}) = A^2 B^2 - (\mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{B})^2$$

Therefore,

$$T_{\text{rot}} = \frac{1}{2} \sum_{\alpha} m_\alpha \left[ \omega^2 r_\alpha^2 - (\boldsymbol{\omega} \cdot \mathbf{r}_\alpha)^2 \right]$$
*(11.7)*

We now express $T_{\text{rot}}$ by using the components $\omega_i$ and $r_{\alpha i}$ of the vectors $\boldsymbol{\omega}$ and $\mathbf{r}_\alpha$. We note that in the body system, $r_\alpha^2 = \sum_i r_{\alpha i}^2$, so we can write:

$$T_{\text{rot}} = \frac{1}{2} \sum_{\alpha} m_\alpha \left[ \left( \sum_i \omega_i^2 \right) \left( \sum_k r_{\alpha k}^2 \right) - \left( \sum_i \omega_i r_{\alpha i} \right) \left( \sum_j \omega_j r_{\alpha j} \right) \right]$$
*(11.8)*

Using the Kronecker delta $\delta_{ij}$, we can write $\omega_i = \sum_j \omega_j \delta_{ij}$, which allows us to rewrite $T_{\text{rot}}$ as:

$$T_{\text{rot}} = \frac{1}{2} \sum_{\alpha} m_\alpha \sum_{i,j} \left( \omega_j \delta_{ij} \omega_i \sum_k r_{\alpha k}^2 - \omega_i \omega_j r_{\alpha i} r_{\alpha j} \right)$$
$$T_{\text{rot}} = \frac{1}{2} \sum_{i,j} \omega_i \omega_j \sum_{\alpha} m_\alpha \left( \delta_{ij} \sum_k r_{\alpha k}^2 - r_{\alpha i} r_{\alpha j} \right)$$
*(11.9)*

If we define the $ij$-th element of the sum over $\alpha$ to be $I_{ij}$:

$$I_{ij} \equiv \sum_{\alpha} m_\alpha \left( \delta_{ij} \sum_k r_{\alpha k}^2 - r_{\alpha i} r_{\alpha j} \right)$$
*(11.10)*

then we have

$$T_{\text{rot}} = \frac{1}{2} \sum_{i,j} I_{ij} \omega_i \omega_j$$
*(11.11)*

If the rotation is about a single axis, this equation in its most restricted form becomes

$$T_{\text{rot}} = \frac{1}{2} I \omega^2$$
*(11.12)*

where $I$ is the scalar rotational inertia (moment of inertia) about the axis of rotation. This equation is the familiar expression for the rotational kinetic energy.

The nine terms $I_{ij}$ constitute the elements of a quantity we designate by $\{\mathbf{I}\}$. In form, $\{\mathbf{I}\}$ is similar to a $3 \times 3$ matrix:

$$\{\mathbf{I}\} = \begin{pmatrix}
\sum_\alpha m_\alpha (r_{\alpha 2}^2 + r_{\alpha 3}^2) & -\sum_\alpha m_\alpha r_{\alpha 1} r_{\alpha 2} & -\sum_\alpha m_\alpha r_{\alpha 1} r_{\alpha 3} \\
- \sum_\alpha m_\alpha r_{\alpha 2} r_{\alpha 1} & \sum_\alpha m_\alpha (r_{\alpha 1}^2 + r_{\alpha 3}^2) & -\sum_\alpha m_\alpha r_{\alpha 2} r_{\alpha 3} \\
- \sum_\alpha m_\alpha r_{\alpha 3} r_{\alpha 1} & -\sum_\alpha m_\alpha r_{\alpha 3} r_{\alpha 2} & \sum_\alpha m_\alpha (r_{\alpha 1}^2 + r_{\alpha 2}^2)
\end{pmatrix}$$
*(11.13a)*

By using components $(x, y, z)$ instead of $(r_1, r_2, r_3)$ and letting $r^2 = x^2 + y^2 + z^2$, Equation 11.13a can be written as:

$$\{\mathbf{I}\} = \begin{pmatrix}
\sum_\alpha m_\alpha (y_\alpha^2 + z_\alpha^2) & -\sum_\alpha m_\alpha x_\alpha y_\alpha & -\sum_\alpha m_\alpha x_\alpha z_\alpha \\
-\sum_\alpha m_\alpha y_\alpha x_\alpha & \sum_\alpha m_\alpha (x_\alpha^2 + z_\alpha^2) & -\sum_\alpha m_\alpha y_\alpha z_\alpha \\
-\sum_\alpha m_\alpha z_\alpha x_\alpha & -\sum_\alpha m_\alpha z_\alpha y_\alpha & \sum_\alpha m_\alpha (x_\alpha^2 + y_\alpha^2)
\end{pmatrix}$$
*(11.13b)*

which is less imposing and more recognizable. We continue, however, with the $r_{i}$ notation because of its mathematical utility.

The diagonal elements $I_{11}$, $I_{22}$, and $I_{33}$ are called the moments of inertia about the $x_1$, $x_2$, and $x_3$ axes, respectively, and the negatives of the off-diagonal elements $I_{12}$, $I_{13}$, etc., are termed the products of inertia.[^3] The inertia tensor is symmetric:

$$I_{ij} = I_{ji}$$
*(11.14)*

and, therefore, there are only six independent elements in $\{\mathbf{I}\}$. Furthermore, the inertia tensor is composed of additive elements; the inertia tensor for a body can be considered to be the sum of the tensors for the various portions of the body. Therefore, if we consider a body as a continuous distribution of matter with mass density $\rho = \rho(\mathbf{r})$, then

$$I_{ij} = \int_{V} \rho(\mathbf{r}) \left( r^2 \delta_{ij} - r_i r_j \right) dV$$
*(11.15)*

where $dV = dx_1\,dx_2\,dx_3$ is the element of volume at the position defined by the vector $\mathbf{r}$, and where $V$ is the volume of the body.

**EXAMPLE 11.3** Calculate the inertia tensor of a homogeneous cube of density $\rho$, mass $M$, and side of length $b$. Let one corner be at the origin, and let three adjacent edges lie along the coordinate axes (Figure 11-3).

![Figure 11-3](file:///c:/Users/Mester/Desktop/semester%204/AM%20II/exam/textbook/M-Chapter-11/09.png)
**Figure 11-3 Context:** Example 11.3. A homogeneous cube of sides $b$ with the origin at one corner.

**Solution.** According to Equation 11.15, we have:

$$I_{11} = \rho \int_{0}^{b} dx_1 \int_{0}^{b} dx_2 \int_{0}^{b} dx_3 \left( x_2^2 + x_3^2 \right) = \rho \cdot b \cdot \left[ \frac{1}{3} x_2^3 \Big|_0^b \cdot b + b \cdot \frac{1}{3} x_3^3 \Big|_0^b \right]$$
$$I_{11} = \rho b \left( \frac{1}{3} b^4 + \frac{1}{3} b^4 \right) = \frac{2}{3} \rho b^5 = \frac{2}{3} M b^2$$

where we have used $M = \rho b^3$. For the off-diagonal elements, we have:

$$I_{12} = -\rho \int_{0}^{b} x_1\,dx_1 \int_{0}^{b} x_2\,dx_2 \int_{0}^{b} dx_3 = -\rho \left( \frac{b^2}{2} \right) \left( \frac{b^2}{2} \right) b = -\frac{1}{4} \rho b^5 = -\frac{1}{4} M b^2$$

It is easy to see that all the diagonal elements are equal and all the off-diagonal elements are equal. If we define $\beta \equiv M b^2$, we have:

$$I_{11} = I_{22} = I_{33} = \frac{2}{3}\beta$$
$$I_{12} = I_{21} = I_{13} = I_{31} = I_{23} = I_{32} = -\frac{1}{4}\beta$$

The moment-of-inertia tensor then becomes:

$$\{\mathbf{I}\} = \beta \begin{pmatrix} 2/3 & -1/4 & -1/4 \\ -1/4 & 2/3 & -1/4 \\ -1/4 & -1/4 & 2/3 \end{pmatrix}$$

We shall continue the investigation of the moment-of-inertia tensor for the cube in later sections.

[^3]: Introduced by Huygens in 1673; Euler coined the name.
