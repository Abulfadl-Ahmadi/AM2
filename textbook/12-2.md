## 12.2 Two Coupled Harmonic Oscillators

### Original Transcribed Notes

A physical example of a coupled system is a solid in which the atoms interact by elastic forces between each other and oscillate about their equilibrium positions. Springs between the atoms represent the elastic forces. A molecule composed of a few such interacting atoms would be an even simpler model. We begin by considering a similar system of coupled motion in one dimension: two masses connected by a spring to each other and by springs to fixed positions (Figure 12-1). We return to this example throughout the chapter as we describe various instances of coupled motion.

We let each of the oscillator springs have a force constant[^2] $\kappa$; the force constant of the coupling spring is $\kappa_{12}$. We restrict the motion to the line connecting the masses, so the system has only two degrees of freedom, represented by the coordinates $x_1$ and $x_2$. Each coordinate is measured from the position of equilibrium.

![Figure 12-1](file:///c:/Users/Mester/Desktop/semester%204/AM%20II/exam/textbook/M-Chapter-12/02.png)
**Figure 12-1 Context:** Two masses are connected by a spring to each other and by springs to fixed positions. This is a system of coupled motion in one dimension.

If $m_1$ and $m_2$ are displaced from their equilibrium positions by amounts $x_1$ and $x_2$ respectively, the force on $m_1$ is $-\kappa x_1 - \kappa_{12}(x_1 - x_2)$, and the force on $m_2$ is $-\kappa x_2 - \kappa_{12}(x_2 - x_1)$. Therefore, the equations of motion are

$$m\ddot{x}_1 + (\kappa + \kappa_{12})x_1 - \kappa_{12}x_2 = 0$$
$$m\ddot{x}_2 + (\kappa + \kappa_{12})x_2 - \kappa_{12}x_1 = 0$$
*(12.1)*

Because we expect the motion to be oscillatory, we attempt a solution of the form

$$x_1(t) = B_1 e^{i\omega t}$$
$$x_2(t) = B_2 e^{i\omega t}$$
*(12.2)*

where the frequency $\omega$ is to be determined and where the amplitudes $B_1$ and $B_2$ may be complex.[^3] These trial solutions are complex functions. Thus, in the final step of the solution, the real parts of $x_1(t)$ and $x_2(t)$ will be taken, because the real part is all that is physically significant. We use this method of solution because of its great efficiency, and we use it again later, leaving out most of the details. Substituting these expressions for the displacements into the equations of motion, we find

$$-m\omega^2 B_1 e^{i\omega t} + (\kappa + \kappa_{12}) B_1 e^{i\omega t} - \kappa_{12} B_2 e^{i\omega t} = 0$$
$$-m\omega^2 B_2 e^{i\omega t} + (\kappa + \kappa_{12}) B_2 e^{i\omega t} - \kappa_{12} B_1 e^{i\omega t} = 0$$
*(12.3)*

Collecting terms and canceling the common exponential factor, we obtain

$$(\kappa + \kappa_{12} - m\omega^2) B_1 - \kappa_{12} B_2 = 0$$
$$-\kappa_{12} B_1 + (\kappa + \kappa_{12} - m\omega^2) B_2 = 0$$
*(12.4)*

For a nontrivial solution to exist for this pair of simultaneous equations, the determinant of the coefficients of $B_1$ and $B_2$ must vanish:

$$\begin{vmatrix} \kappa + \kappa_{12} - m\omega^2 & -\kappa_{12} \\ -\kappa_{12} & \kappa + \kappa_{12} - m\omega^2 \end{vmatrix} = 0$$
*(12.5)*

The expansion of this secular determinant yields

$$(\kappa + \kappa_{12} - m\omega^2)^2 - \kappa_{12}^2 = 0$$
*(12.6)*

Hence,

$$\kappa + \kappa_{12} - m\omega^2 = \pm \kappa_{12}$$

Solving for $\omega$, we obtain

$$\omega_1 = \sqrt{\frac{\kappa}{m}}$$
*(12.7)*

$$\omega_2 = \sqrt{\frac{\kappa + 2\kappa_{12}}{m}}$$
*(12.8)*

We therefore have two characteristic frequencies (or eigenfrequencies) for the system. Thus, the general solution to the problem is

$$x_1(t) = B_{11} e^{i\omega_1 t} + B_{11}' e^{-i\omega_1 t} + B_{12} e^{i\omega_2 t} + B_{12}' e^{-i\omega_2 t}$$
$$x_2(t) = B_{21} e^{i\omega_1 t} + B_{21}' e^{-i\omega_1 t} + B_{22} e^{i\omega_2 t} + B_{22}' e^{-i\omega_2 t}$$
*(12.9)*

where we have explicitly written both positive and negative frequencies, because the radicals in Equations 12.7 and 12.8 can carry either sign.

In Equation 12.9, the amplitudes are not all independent, as we may verify by substituting $\omega_1$ and $\omega_2$ into Equation 12.4. We find:
- For $\omega = \omega_1$: $B_{21} = B_{11}$ and $B_{21}' = B_{11}'$
- For $\omega = \omega_2$: $B_{22} = -B_{12}$ and $B_{22}' = -B_{12}'$

The only subscripts necessary on the $B$'s are those indicating the particular eigenfrequency (i.e., the second subscripts). We can therefore write the general solution as

$$x_1(t) = B_1^+ e^{i\omega_1 t} + B_1^- e^{-i\omega_1 t} + B_2^+ e^{i\omega_2 t} + B_2^- e^{-i\omega_2 t}$$
$$x_2(t) = B_1^+ e^{i\omega_1 t} + B_1^- e^{-i\omega_1 t} - B_2^+ e^{i\omega_2 t} - B_2^- e^{-i\omega_2 t}$$
*(12.10)*

Thus, we have four arbitrary constants in the general solution—just as we expect—because we have two equations of motion that are of second order.

We mentioned earlier that we could always define a set of coordinates that have a simple time dependence and that correspond to the excitation of the various oscillation modes of the system. Let us examine the pair of coordinates defined by

$$\eta_1 = x_1 + x_2$$
*(12.11)*

$$\eta_2 = x_1 - x_2$$

or

$$x_1 = \frac{1}{2}(\eta_1 + \eta_2)$$
$$x_2 = \frac{1}{2}(\eta_1 - \eta_2)$$
*(12.12)*

Substituting these expressions for $x_1$ and $x_2$ into Equation 12.1, we find

$$m(\ddot{\eta}_1 + \ddot{\eta}_2) + (\kappa + \kappa_{12})(\eta_1 + \eta_2) - \kappa_{12}(\eta_1 - \eta_2) = 0$$
$$m(\ddot{\eta}_1 - \ddot{\eta}_2) + (\kappa + \kappa_{12})(\eta_1 - \eta_2) - \kappa_{12}(\eta_1 + \eta_2) = 0$$
*(12.13)*

which can be solved (by adding and subtracting) to yield

$$m\ddot{\eta}_1 + \kappa\eta_1 = 0$$
$$m\ddot{\eta}_2 + (\kappa + 2\kappa_{12})\eta_2 = 0$$
*(12.14)*

The coordinates $\eta_1$ and $\eta_2$ are now uncoupled and are therefore independent. The solutions are

$$\eta_1(t) = C_1^+ e^{i\omega_1 t} + C_1^- e^{-i\omega_1 t}$$
$$\eta_2(t) = C_2^+ e^{i\omega_2 t} + C_2^- e^{-i\omega_2 t}$$
*(12.15)*

where the frequencies $\omega_1$ and $\omega_2$ are given by Equations 12.7 and 12.8. Thus, $\eta_1$ and $\eta_2$ are the normal coordinates of the problem. In a later section, we establish a general method for obtaining the normal coordinates.

![Figure 12-2](file:///c:/Users/Mester/Desktop/semester%204/AM%20II/exam/textbook/M-Chapter-12/05.png)
**Figure 12-2 Context:** The two characteristic frequencies are indicated schematically. One is the antisymmetrical mode (masses are out of phase) and the other is the symmetrical mode (masses are in phase).

If we impose the special initial conditions $x_1(0) = -x_2(0)$ and $\dot{x}_1(0) = -\dot{x}_2(0)$, we find $\eta_1(0) = 0$ and $\dot{\eta}_1(0) = 0$, which leads to $C_1^+ = C_1^- = 0$; that is, $\eta_1(t) = 0$ for all values of $t$. Thus, the particles oscillate always out of phase and with frequency $\omega_2$; this is the antisymmetrical mode of oscillation. However, if we begin with $x_1(0) = x_2(0)$ and $\dot{x}_1(0) = \dot{x}_2(0)$, we find $\eta_2(t) = 0$, and the particles oscillate in phase and with frequency $\omega_1$; this is the symmetrical mode of oscillation. These results are illustrated schematically in Figure 12-2. The general motion of the system is a linear combination of the symmetrical and antisymmetrical modes.

The fact that the antisymmetrical mode has the higher frequency and the symmetrical mode has the lower frequency is actually a general result. In a complex system of linearly coupled oscillators, the mode possessing the highest degree of symmetry has the lowest frequency. If the symmetry is destroyed, then the springs must "work harder" in the antisymmetrical modes, and the frequency is raised.

Notice that if we were to hold $m_2$ fixed and allow $m_1$ to oscillate, the frequency would be $\sqrt{(\kappa + \kappa_{12})/m}$. We would obtain the same result for the frequency of oscillation of $m_2$ if $m_1$ were held fixed. The oscillators are identical and in the absence of coupling have the same oscillation frequency. The effect of coupling is to separate the common frequency, with one characteristic frequency becoming larger and one becoming smaller than the frequency for uncoupled motion. If we denote by $\omega_0$ the frequency for uncoupled motion, then $\omega_2 > \omega_0 > \omega_1$, and we may schematically indicate the effect of the coupling as in Figure 12-3a. The solution for the characteristic frequencies in the problem of three coupled identical masses is illustrated in Figure 12-3b. Again, we have a splitting of the characteristic frequencies, with one greater and one smaller than $\omega_0$. This is a general result: For an even number $n$ of identical nearest neighbor coupled oscillators, $n/2$ characteristic frequencies are greater than $\omega_0$ and $n/2$ characteristic frequencies are smaller than $\omega_0$. If $n$ is odd, one characteristic frequency is equal to $\omega_0$, and the remaining $n - 1$ characteristic frequencies are symmetrically distributed above and below $\omega_0$. The reader familiar with the phenomenon of the Zeeman effect in atomic spectra will appreciate the similarity with this result: In each case, there is a symmetrical splitting of the frequency caused by the introduction of an interaction (in one case by the application of a magnetic field and in the other by the coupling of particles through the intermediary of the springs).

**Figure 12-3 Context:** (a) Coupling separates the common frequency for two identical masses, with one characteristic frequency being higher and one being lower than the frequency $\omega_0$ for uncoupled motion. (b) For three coupled identical masses, one characteristic frequency is smaller than $\omega_0$ and one is larger. For $n$ (number of oscillators) odd, one characteristic frequency is equal to $\omega_0$. The separations are only schematic.

[^2]: Henceforth, we denote force constants by $\kappa$ rather than by $k$ as heretofore. The symbol $k$ is reserved for (beginning in Chapter 13) an entirely different context.
[^3]: Because a complex amplitude has a magnitude and a phase, we have the two arbitrary constants necessary in the solution of a second-order differential equation; that is, we could equally well write $x(t) = |B| \exp [i(\omega t - \delta)]$ or $x(t) = |B| \cos (\omega t - \delta)$, as in Equation 3.6b. Later (see Equation 12.9), we shall find it more convenient to use two distinct real amplitudes and the time-varying factors $\exp(i\omega t)$ and $\exp(-i\omega t)$. These various forms of solution are all entirely equivalent.
