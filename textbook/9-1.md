## 9.1 Introduction

### Original Transcribed Notes

Thus far, we have treated our dynamical problems primarily in terms of single particles. Even though we have considered extended objects such as projectiles and planets, we have been able to treat them as single particles. Generally, we have not had to deal with the internal interactions between the many particles that make up the extended body. Later, when we treat the dynamics of rigid bodies, we must describe rotational as well as translational motion. We need to prepare the techniques that will allow us to do this. We first extend our discussion to describe the system of n particles. These particles may form a loose aggregate-such as a pile of rocks or a volume of gas molecules or form a rigid body in which the constituent particles are restrained from moving relative to one another.

We devote the latter part of the chapter to a study of the interaction of two particles ($n=2$). For the three-body problem ($n=3$), the solutions become formidable. Perturbation techniques often are used, although great progress has been made through the use of numerical methods with high-speed computers. Finally, we shall examine rocket motion.

Newton's Third Law plays a prominent role in the dynamics of a system of particles because of the internal forces between the particles in the system. We need to make two assumptions concerning the internal forces:

- The forces exerted by two particles $\alpha$ and $\beta$ on each other are equal in magnitude and opposite in direction.
    
- Let $f_{\alpha\beta}$ represent the force on the $\alpha$th particle due to the $\beta$th particle.
    
- The so-called "weak" form of Newton's Third Law is $f_{\alpha\beta}=-f_{\beta\alpha}$.
    
- The forces exerted by two particles $\alpha$ and $\beta$ on each other, in addition to being equal and opposite, must lie on the straight line joining the two particles.
    
- This more restrictive form of Newton's Third Law, often called the "strong" form, is displayed in Figure 9-1.
    

**Figure 9-1 Context:** Example of the strong form of Newton's Third Law, where the equal and opposite forces between two particles must lie along a straight line joining the two particles. The force is attractive, as in the molecular attraction in a solid.

We must be careful to remember when each form of Newton's Third Law applies. We recall from Section 2.2 that the Third Law is not always valid for moving charged particles ; electromagnetic forces are velocity dependent. For example, magnetic forces, those forces exerted on a moving charge $q$ in a magnetic field $B$ ($F=qv\times B$), obey the weak form, but not the strong form, of the Third Law.

### **Elaboration: Expanding on the Concepts**

> **From Single Particles to $n$-Body Systems:**
> 
> In introductory physics, treating macroscopic objects (like a baseball or a planet) as single point masses works perfectly for analyzing purely _translational_ motion (movement through space). However, this simplification fails when an object rotates, flexes, or breaks apart. By modeling an object as a "system of $n$ particles," we can account for the internal distribution of mass. This is the foundational step required to transition from basic point-mass kinematics to the complex dynamics of rigid bodies (like gyroscopes) and deformable bodies (like fluids or gases).
> 
> **The Notorious "Three-Body Problem":**
> 
> The text mentions that for $n=3$, solutions become "formidable." This is an understatement in physics. While the two-body problem (e.g., Earth orbiting the Sun) can be solved exactly to yield neat elliptical orbits using classical mechanics, adding just one more mass (like the Moon) creates a system with chaotic, non-linear dynamics. Henri Poincaré proved in the late 19th century that there is no general closed-form analytical solution for the three-body problem. This is why physicists must rely on **perturbation theory** (approximating the third body's gravitational pull as a slight disturbance to a two-body system) or computationally heavy **numerical integration** to predict orbits.
> 
> **Newton's Third Law: Weak vs. Strong Forms**
> 
> The distinction between the weak and strong forms of Newton's Third Law is crucial for understanding conservation laws in physics:
> 
> - **The Weak Form ($f_{\alpha\beta}=-f_{\beta\alpha}$):** This simply states that forces occur in equal and opposite pairs. This form alone is sufficient to prove the **Conservation of Linear Momentum** for a system, because when you sum up all internal forces, they cancel each other out perfectly.
>     
> - **The Strong Form (Central Forces):** This adds the geometric constraint that the force vectors must point exactly along the imaginary line connecting the two particles. Gravity and electrostatic (Coulomb) forces obey this. The strong form is required to prove the **Conservation of Angular Momentum** for a system. If internal forces were equal and opposite but _not_ collinear, they would create a net internal torque, causing the object to spontaneously spin on its own—which violates the laws of physics.
>     
> 
> **When the Strong Form Fails (Electrodynamics):**
> 
> The text rightly points out that magnetic forces violate the strong form. Because the magnetic force on a moving charge is determined by the cross product $F=qv\times B$, the force is perpendicular to the velocity vector. If two charged particles are moving parallel to each other, the magnetic forces they exert on one another are equal and opposite (weak form), but they do not point along the line connecting them. Therefore, interacting magnetic fields contain their own angular momentum, which must be factored in to satisfy conservation laws!
