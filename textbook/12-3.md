## 12.3 Weak Coupling

### Original Transcribed Notes

Some of the more interesting cases of coupled oscillations occur when the coupling is weak—that is, when the force constant of the coupling spring is small compared with that of the oscillator springs: $\kappa_{12} \ll \kappa$. According to Equations 12.7 and 12.8, the frequencies $\omega_1$ and $\omega_2$ are

$$\omega_1 = \sqrt{\frac{\kappa}{m}} \equiv \omega_0$$
*(12.16)*

$$\omega_2 = \sqrt{\frac{\kappa + 2\kappa_{12}}{m}}$$

If the coupling is weak, we may expand the expression for $\omega_2$:

$$\omega_2 = \sqrt{\frac{\kappa}{m}} \sqrt{1 + \frac{2\kappa_{12}}{\kappa}} \approx \omega_0 \left( 1 + \frac{\kappa_{12}}{\kappa} \right)$$
*(12.17)*

Let us define a small parameter $\epsilon$ as

$$\epsilon \equiv \frac{\kappa_{12}}{2\kappa} \ll 1$$
*(12.18)*

Then we can write

$$\omega_2 \approx \omega_0(1 + 2\epsilon)$$
*(12.19)*

$$\omega_1 = \omega_0$$
*(12.20)*

Therefore, the difference and sum of the two characteristic frequencies are given approximately by

$$\frac{\omega_2 - \omega_1}{2} \approx \epsilon \omega_0$$
$$\frac{\omega_2 + \omega_1}{2} \approx \omega_0(1 + \epsilon) \approx \omega_0$$
*(12.21)*

We can now examine the way a weakly coupled system behaves. If we displace Oscillator 1 a distance $D$ and release it from rest, the initial conditions for the system are

$$x_1(0) = D, \quad x_2(0) = 0, \quad \dot{x}_1(0) = 0, \quad \dot{x}_2(0) = 0$$
*(12.22)*

If we substitute these initial conditions into Equation 12.10 for $x_1(t)$ and $x_2(t)$, we find the amplitudes to be

$$B_1^+ = B_1^- = B_2^+ = B_2^- = \frac{D}{4}$$
*(12.23)*

Then, $x_1(t)$ becomes

$$x_1(t) = \frac{D}{4} \left[ e^{i\omega_1 t} + e^{-i\omega_1 t} + e^{i\omega_2 t} + e^{-i\omega_2 t} \right]$$
$$x_1(t) = \frac{D}{2} (\cos \omega_1 t + \cos \omega_2 t)$$
$$x_1(t) = D \cos \left( \frac{\omega_2 - \omega_1}{2} t \right) \cos \left( \frac{\omega_2 + \omega_1}{2} t \right)$$
*(12.24)*

Using the approximations in Equation 12.21, we have

$$\frac{\omega_2 - \omega_1}{2} \approx \epsilon \omega_0, \quad \frac{\omega_2 + \omega_1}{2} \approx \omega_0$$
*(12.25)*

Therefore,[^4]

$$x_1(t) \approx \left( D \cos \epsilon \omega_0 t \right) \cos \omega_0 t$$
*(12.26a)*

Similarly,

$$x_2(t) \approx \left( D \sin \epsilon \omega_0 t \right) \sin \omega_0 t$$
*(12.26b)*

Because $\epsilon$ is small, the quantities $D \cos \epsilon \omega_0 t$ and $D \sin \epsilon \omega_0 t$ vary slowly with time. Therefore, $x_1(t)$ and $x_2(t)$ are essentially sinusoidal functions with slowly varying amplitudes. Although only $x_1$ is initially different from zero, as time increases the amplitude of $x_1$ decreases slowly with time, and the amplitude of $x_2$ increases slowly from zero. Hence, energy is transferred from the first oscillator to the second. When $t = \pi / (2\epsilon \omega_0)$, then $D \cos \epsilon \omega_0 t = 0$, and all the energy has been transferred. As time increases further, energy is transferred back to the first oscillator. This is the familiar phenomenon of beats and is illustrated in Figure 12-4. (In the case illustrated, $\epsilon = 0.08$.)
