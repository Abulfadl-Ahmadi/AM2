## 12.6 Normal Coordinates

> [!abstract]
> **Concept Hub:** [[Chapter 12 - Coupled Oscillations and Normal Modes]] | **Textbook Index:** [[Textbook Ch 12 - Coupled Oscillations and Normal Modes]] | [[Brain-Map|🧠 Brain Map]]

### Original Transcribed Notes

As we have seen (Equation 12.43), the general solution for the motion of the coordinate $q_j$ must be a sum over terms, each of which depends on an individual eigenfrequency. In the previous section, we showed that the vectors $\mathbf{a}_r$ are orthogonal (Equation 12.55) and, as a matter of convenience, we even normalized their components $a_{jr}$ (Equation 12.57) to arrive at Equation 12.58; that is, we have removed all ambiguity in the solution for the $a_{jr}$, so it is no longer possible to specify an arbitrary displacement for a particle. Because such a restriction is not physically meaningful, we must introduce a constant scale factor $\alpha_r$ (which depends on the initial conditions of the problem) to account for the loss of generality introduced by the arbitrary normalization. Thus,

$$q_j(t) = \sum_r \alpha_r a_{jr} e^{i(\omega_r t - \delta_r)}$$
*(12.60)*

To simplify the notation, we write[^11]

$$q_j(t) = \sum_r a_{jr} \eta_r(t)$$
*(12.61)*

where we define the normal coordinates $\eta_r(t)$ by

$$\eta_r(t) = \beta_r e^{i\omega_r t}$$
*(12.62)*

where the quantities $\beta_r$ are new (complex) scale factors that incorporate the phases $\delta_r$.

The $\eta_r$, by definition, are quantities that undergo oscillation at only one frequency. They may be considered as new coordinates, called normal coordinates, for the system. The $\eta_r$ satisfy equations of the form

$$\ddot{\eta}_r + \omega_r^2 \eta_r = 0$$
*(12.63)*

There are $n$ independent such equations, so the equations of motion expressed in normal coordinates become completely separable.

---

**EXAMPLE 12.2** Derive Equation 12.63 directly by using Lagrange's equations of motion.

**Solution.** We note that from Equation 12.61,

$$\dot{q}_j = \sum_r a_{jr} \dot{\eta}_r$$

and from Equation 12.34 we have, for the kinetic energy,

$$T = \frac{1}{2} \sum_{j,k} m_{jk} \dot{q}_j \dot{q}_k = \frac{1}{2} \sum_{j,k} m_{jk} \left( \sum_r a_{jr} \dot{\eta}_r \right) \left( \sum_s a_{ks} \dot{\eta}_s \right) = \frac{1}{2} \sum_{r,s} \dot{\eta}_r \dot{\eta}_s \left( \sum_{j,k} m_{jk} a_{jr} a_{ks} \right)$$

The sum in the parentheses is just $\delta_{rs}$, according to the orthonormality condition (Equation 12.58). Therefore,

$$T = \frac{1}{2} \sum_r \dot{\eta}_r^2$$
*(12.64)*

Similarly, from Equation 12.34 we have for the potential energy,

$$U = \frac{1}{2} \sum_{j,k} A_{jk} q_j q_k = \frac{1}{2} \sum_{j,k} A_{jk} \left( \sum_r a_{jr} \eta_r \right) \left( \sum_s a_{ks} \eta_s \right) = \frac{1}{2} \sum_{r,s} \eta_r \eta_s \left( \sum_{j,k} A_{jk} a_{jr} a_{ks} \right)$$

From Equation 12.51, we have

$$\sum_k A_{jk} a_{ks} = \omega_s^2 \sum_k m_{jk} a_{ks}$$

Multiplying by $a_{jr}$ and summing over $j$, we find

$$\sum_{j,k} A_{jk} a_{jr} a_{ks} = \omega_s^2 \sum_{j,k} m_{jk} a_{jr} a_{ks} = \omega_s^2 \delta_{rs}$$

so the potential energy becomes

$$U = \frac{1}{2} \sum_r \omega_r^2 \eta_r^2$$
*(12.65)*

Using Equations 12.64 and 12.65, the Lagrangian is

$$L = T - U = \frac{1}{2} \sum_r (\dot{\eta}_r^2 - \omega_r^2 \eta_r^2)$$
*(12.66)*

and Lagrange's equations are

$$\frac{d}{dt}\left(\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{\eta}_r}\right) - \frac{\partial L}{\partial \eta_r} = 0$$

which yields

$$\ddot{\eta}_r + \omega_r^2 \eta_r = 0$$

as found in Equation 12.63.

---

Thus, when the configuration of a system is expressed in normal coordinates, both the potential and kinetic energies become simultaneously diagonal. Because it is the off-diagonal elements of $\{\mathbf{m}\}$ and $\{\mathbf{A}\}$ that give rise to the coupling of the particles' motions, it should be evident that a choice of coordinates that renders these tensors diagonal uncouples the coordinates and makes the problem completely separable into the independent motions of the normal coordinates, each with its particular normal frequency.[^12]

The foregoing has been a mathematical description of the methods used to determine the characteristic frequencies $\omega_r$ and to describe the coordinates $\eta_r$ of the normal mode motion. The actual application of the method can be summarized by several steps:

1. Choose generalized coordinates and find $T$ and $U$ in the normal Lagrangian method. This corresponds to using Equations 12.34.
2. Represent $A_{jk}$ and $m_{jk}$ as tensors in $n \times n$ arrays, and use Equation 12.42 to determine the $n$ values of eigenfrequencies $\omega_r$.
3. For each value of $\omega_r$, determine the ratios $a_{1r} : a_{2r} : \dots : a_{nr}$ by substituting into Equation 12.40:
   $$\sum_j (A_{jk} - \omega_r^2 m_{jk}) a_{jr} = 0$$
   *(12.67)*
4. If needed, determine the scale factors $\beta_r$ (Equation 12.61) from the initial conditions.
5. Determine the normal coordinates $\eta_r$ by appropriate linear combinations of the $q_j$ coordinates that display oscillations at the single eigenfrequency $\omega_r$. The description of motion for this single normal coordinate $\eta_r$ is called a normal mode. The general motion (Equation 12.61) of the system is a complicated superposition of the normal modes.

We now apply these steps in several examples.

---

**EXAMPLE 12.3** Determine the eigenfrequencies, eigenvectors, and normal coordinates of the mass-spring example in Section 12.2 by using the procedure just described. Assume $\kappa_{12} \sim \kappa$.

**Solution.** The eigenfrequencies were determined in Example 12.1, where we found $T$ and $U$ (step 1). We can find the components for $A_{jk}$ directly from Equation 12.46 or by inspection from Equation 12.45, making sure $\{\mathbf{A}\}$ is symmetrical:

$$\{\mathbf{A}\} = \begin{pmatrix} \kappa + \kappa_{12} & -\kappa_{12} \\ -\kappa_{12} & \kappa + \kappa_{12} \end{pmatrix}$$
*(12.68)*

The array $m_{jk}$ can easily be determined from Equation 12.47:

$$\{\mathbf{m}\} = \begin{pmatrix} m & 0 \\ 0 & m \end{pmatrix}$$
*(12.69)*

We use Equation 12.42 to determine the eigenfrequencies $\omega_r$:

$$\begin{vmatrix} \kappa + \kappa_{12} - m\omega^2 & -\kappa_{12} \\ -\kappa_{12} & \kappa + \kappa_{12} - m\omega^2 \end{vmatrix} = 0$$

which is identical to Equation 12.50 with the results of Equation 12.8 for $\omega_1$ and $\omega_2$.

We use Equation 12.67 to determine the eigenvector components $a_{jr}$. We have two equations for each value of $r$, but because we can determine only the ratios $a_{1r}/a_{2r}$, one equation for each $r$ is sufficient. For $r = 1$, $k = 1$, we have

$$(A_{11} - \omega_1^2 m_{11}) a_{11} + (A_{21} - \omega_1^2 m_{21}) a_{21} = 0$$
*(12.70)*

or, inserting the values for $A_{11}$, $A_{21}$, $\omega_1^2$, and $m_{jk}$, and using the simplification that $\kappa_{12} \sim \kappa$,

$$(\kappa + \kappa_{12} - \kappa) a_{11} - \kappa_{12} a_{21} = 0$$

with the result

$$a_{11} = a_{21}$$
*(12.71)*

For $r = 2$, $k = 1$, we have

$$(A_{11} - \omega_2^2 m_{11}) a_{12} + (A_{21} - \omega_2^2 m_{21}) a_{22} = 0$$

Inserting the value $\omega_2^2 = (\kappa + 2\kappa_{12})/m$, we get

$$[\kappa + \kappa_{12} - (\kappa + 2\kappa_{12})] a_{12} - \kappa_{12} a_{22} = 0 \implies -\kappa_{12} a_{12} - \kappa_{12} a_{22} = 0$$

with the result

$$a_{12} = -a_{22}$$
*(12.72)*

The general motion (Equation 12.61) becomes

$$x_1(t) = a_{11} \eta_1(t) + a_{12} \eta_2(t)$$
$$x_2(t) = a_{21} \eta_1(t) + a_{22} \eta_2(t)$$
*(12.73)*

Using Equations 12.71 and 12.72, this becomes

$$x_1(t) = a_{11} (\eta_1(t) + \eta_2(t))$$
$$x_2(t) = a_{11} (\eta_1(t) - \eta_2(t))$$
*(12.74)*

where we have chosen $a_{11} = a_{12}$ for convenience.

Adding $x_1$ and $x_2$ gives

$$\eta_1(t) = \frac{1}{2a_{11}} (x_1(t) + x_2(t))$$
*(12.75)*

Subtracting $x_2$ from $x_1$ gives

$$\eta_2(t) = \frac{1}{2a_{11}} (x_1(t) - x_2(t))$$
*(12.76)*

The normal coordinate $\eta_2$ can be determined by finding the conditions when the other normal coordinate $\eta_1$ remains equal to zero. From Equation 12.76, $\eta_2 = 0$ when $x_1 = x_2$. Thus, for normal mode 1 ($\eta_1$), the two masses oscillate in phase (the symmetrical mode). The distance between the particles is always the same, and they oscillate as if the spring connecting them were a rigid, weightless rod.

Similarly, we can find the conditions for the normal coordinate $\eta_1$ by determining when $\eta_2 = 0$ ($x_1 = -x_2$). In normal mode 2 ($\eta_2$), the particles oscillate out of phase (the antisymmetrical mode).

This analysis (summarized in Table 12-1) confirms our previous results (Section 12.2), and the particle motion is as shown in Figure 12-2. Such motions for atoms in molecules are common. Remember that we let $\kappa_{12} \sim \kappa$ during this example.

We may determine the components of the eigenvectors (Equation 12.59),

$$\mathbf{a}_1 = a_{11} \mathbf{e}_1 + a_{21} \mathbf{e}_2$$
$$\mathbf{a}_2 = a_{12} \mathbf{e}_1 + a_{22} \mathbf{e}_2$$
*(12.77)*

by using Equations 12.71 and 12.72:

$$\mathbf{a}_1 = a_{11} (\mathbf{e}_1 + \mathbf{e}_2)$$
$$\mathbf{a}_2 = a_{12} (\mathbf{e}_1 - \mathbf{e}_2)$$
*(12.78)*

Although normally not required, we may determine the values of $a_{11}$ and $a_{12}$ from the orthonormality condition of Equation 12.58:

$$a_{11} = a_{12} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2m}}$$
*(12.79)*

**EXAMPLE 12.4** Determine the eigenfrequencies and describe the normal mode motion for two pendula of equal lengths $l$ and equal masses $m$ connected by a spring of force constant $\kappa$ as shown in Figure 12-5. The spring is unstretched in the equilibrium position.

**Solution.** We choose $\theta_1$ and $\theta_2$ (Figure 12-5) as the generalized coordinates. The potential energy is chosen to be zero in the equilibrium position. The kinetic and potential energies of the system are, for small angles,

$$T = \frac{1}{2} m l^2 \dot{\theta}_1^2 + \frac{1}{2} m l^2 \dot{\theta}_2^2$$
*(12.80)*

$$U = mgl(1 - \cos \theta_1) + mgl(1 - \cos \theta_2) + \frac{1}{2} \kappa (d \sin \theta_1 - d \sin \theta_2)^2$$
*(12.81)*

Using the small oscillation assumptions $\sin \theta \approx \theta$ and $\cos \theta \approx 1 - \theta^2/2$, we can write

$$U \approx \frac{1}{2} mgl (\theta_1^2 + \theta_2^2) + \frac{1}{2} \kappa d^2 (\theta_1 - \theta_2)^2$$
*(12.82)*

The components of $\{\mathbf{A}\}$ and $\{\mathbf{m}\}$ are

$$\{\mathbf{m}\} = \begin{pmatrix} m l^2 & 0 \\ 0 & m l^2 \end{pmatrix}$$
*(12.83)*

$$\{\mathbf{A}\} = \begin{pmatrix} mgl + \kappa d^2 & -\kappa d^2 \\ -\kappa d^2 & mgl + \kappa d^2 \end{pmatrix}$$
*(12.84)*

The determinant needed to find the eigenfrequencies $\omega_r$ is

$$\begin{vmatrix} mgl + \kappa d^2 - \omega^2 m l^2 & -\kappa d^2 \\ -\kappa d^2 & mgl + \kappa d^2 - \omega^2 m l^2 \end{vmatrix} = 0$$
*(12.85)*

which gives the characteristic equation

$$(mgl + \kappa d^2 - \omega^2 m l^2)^2 - (\kappa d^2)^2 = 0$$

$$mgl + \kappa d^2 - \omega^2 m l^2 = \pm \kappa d^2$$
*(12.86)*

Taking the plus sign, $\omega = \omega_1$:

$$mgl + \kappa d^2 - \omega_1^2 m l^2 = \kappa d^2$$

$$\omega_1 = \sqrt{\frac{g}{l}}$$
*(12.87)*

Taking the minus sign in Equation 12.86, $\omega = \omega_2$:

$$mgl + \kappa d^2 - \omega_2^2 m l^2 = -\kappa d^2$$

$$\omega_2 = \sqrt{\frac{g}{l} + \frac{2\kappa d^2}{m l^2}}$$
*(12.88)*

Putting the values of $\omega_1$ and $\omega_2$ into Equation 12.67 gives, for $k = 1$,

$$(mgl + \kappa d^2 - \omega^2 m l^2) a_{1r} - \kappa d^2 a_{2r} = 0$$
*(12.89)*

If $r = 1$, then since $mgl + \kappa d^2 - \omega_1^2 m l^2 = \kappa d^2$,

$$\kappa d^2 a_{11} - \kappa d^2 a_{21} = 0 \implies a_{11} = a_{21}$$
*(12.90)*

If $r = 2$, then since $mgl + \kappa d^2 - \omega_2^2 m l^2 = -\kappa d^2$,

$$-\kappa d^2 a_{12} - \kappa d^2 a_{22} = 0 \implies a_{12} = -a_{22}$$
*(12.91)*

We write the coordinates $\theta_1$ and $\theta_2$ in terms of the normal coordinates by

$$\theta_1(t) = a_{11} \eta_1(t) + a_{12} \eta_2(t)$$
$$\theta_2(t) = a_{21} \eta_1(t) + a_{22} \eta_2(t)$$
*(12.92)*

Using Equations 12.90 and 12.91, and setting $a_{11} = a_{12}$ for convenience, Equations 12.92 become

$$\theta_1(t) = a_{11} (\eta_1(t) + \eta_2(t))$$
$$\theta_2(t) = a_{11} (\eta_1(t) - \eta_2(t))$$
*(12.93)*

The normal coordinates are easily determined, by adding and subtracting $\theta_1$ and $\theta_2$, to be

$$\eta_1 = \frac{1}{2a_{11}} (\theta_1 + \theta_2)$$
$$\eta_2 = \frac{1}{2a_{11}} (\theta_1 - \theta_2)$$
*(12.94)*

Because normal coordinate $\eta_1$ occurs when $\eta_2 = 0$, then $\theta_1 = \theta_2$ for normal mode 1 (symmetrical). Similarly, normal coordinate $\eta_2$ occurs when $\eta_1 = 0$ ($\theta_1 = -\theta_2$), and normal mode 2 is antisymmetrical. The normal mode motions are shown in Figure 12-6. Notice that for mode 1, the spring is neither compressed nor extended. The two pendula merely oscillate in unison with their natural frequencies $\omega_1 = \omega_0 = \sqrt{g/l}$. These motions can be easily demonstrated in the laboratory or classroom. The higher frequency of normal mode 2 is easily displayed for a stiff spring.
