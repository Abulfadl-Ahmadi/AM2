## 11.10 Force-Free Motion of a Symmetric Top

> [!abstract] Vault Links
> **Concept Hub:** [[Chapter 11 - Dynamics of Rigid Bodies]] | **Textbook Index:** [[Textbook Ch 11 - Dynamics of Rigid Bodies]] | [[Brain-Map|🧠 Brain Map]]

### Original Transcribed Notes

If we consider a symmetric top, that is, a rigid body with $I_1 = I_2 \neq I_3$, then the force-free Euler equations (Equation 11.114) become:

$$\begin{aligned}
(I_1 - I_3)\omega_2\omega_3 - I_1\dot{\omega}_1 &= 0 \\
(I_3 - I_1)\omega_3\omega_1 - I_1\dot{\omega}_2 &= 0 \\
I_3\dot{\omega}_3 &= 0
\end{aligned}$$
*(11.128)*

Because for force-free motion the center of mass of the body is either at rest or in uniform motion with respect to the fixed or inertial frame of reference, we can, without loss of generality, specify that the body's center of mass is at rest and located at the origin of the fixed coordinate system. We consider the case in which the angular velocity vector $\boldsymbol{\omega}$ does not lie along a principal axis of the body, otherwise the motion is trivial.

The first result for the motion follows from the third part of Equations 11.128: $I_3\dot{\omega}_3 = 0$, or:

$$\omega_3 = \text{constant}$$
*(11.129)*

The first two parts of Equation 11.128 can be written as:

$$\begin{aligned}
\dot{\omega}_1 &= \left( \frac{I_1 - I_3}{I_1} \omega_3 \right) \omega_2 \\
\dot{\omega}_2 &= -\left( \frac{I_1 - I_3}{I_1} \omega_3 \right) \omega_1
\end{aligned}$$
*(11.130)*

Because the terms in the parentheses are composed of constants, we may define:

$$\Omega \equiv \frac{I_1 - I_3}{I_1} \omega_3$$
*(11.131)*

so that:

$$\begin{aligned}
\dot{\omega}_1 - \Omega \omega_2 &= 0 \\
\dot{\omega}_2 + \Omega \omega_1 &= 0
\end{aligned}$$
*(11.132)*

These are coupled equations of familiar form, and we can effect a solution by multiplying the second equation by $i$ and adding it to the first:

$$(\dot{\omega}_1 + i\dot{\omega}_2) + i\Omega(\omega_1 + i\omega_2) = 0$$
*(11.133)*

If we define a complex variable:

$$\eta \equiv \omega_1 + i\omega_2$$
*(11.134)*

then:

$$\dot{\eta} + i\Omega\eta = 0$$
*(11.135)*

with the solution:[^15]

$$\eta(t) = A e^{-i\Omega t}$$
*(11.136)*

Thus,

$$\omega_1 + i\omega_2 = A\cos\Omega t - iA\sin\Omega t$$
*(11.137)*

and therefore:

$$\begin{aligned}
\omega_1(t) &= A\cos\Omega t \\
\omega_2(t) &= -A\sin\Omega t
\end{aligned}$$
*(11.138)*

Because $\omega_3 = \text{constant}$, we note that the magnitude of $\boldsymbol{\omega}$ is also constant:

$$|\boldsymbol{\omega}| = \omega = \sqrt{\omega_1^2 + \omega_2^2 + \omega_3^2} = \sqrt{A^2 + \omega_3^2} = \text{constant}$$
*(11.139)*

Equations 11.138 are the parametric equations of a circle, so the projection of the vector $\boldsymbol{\omega}$ (which is of constant magnitude) onto the $x_1 - x_2$ plane describes a circle with time (Figure 11-12).

![Figure 11-12](file:///c:/Users/Mester/Desktop/semester%204/AM%20II/exam/textbook/M-Chapter-11/40.png)
**Figure 11-12 Context:** The angular velocity of a force-free symmetric top precesses with constant angular velocity $\Omega$ about the symmetric $x_3$-axis of the body. Thus $\boldsymbol{\omega}$ traces out a cone around the body symmetric axis.

The $x_3$-axis is the symmetry axis of the body, so we find that the angular velocity vector $\boldsymbol{\omega}$ revolves or precesses about the body $x_3$-axis with a constant angular frequency $\Omega$. Thus, to an observer in the body coordinate system, $\boldsymbol{\omega}$ traces out a cone around the body symmetry axis, called the body cone.

Because we are considering force-free motion, the angular momentum vector $\mathbf{L}$ is stationary in the fixed coordinate system and is constant in time. An additional constant of the motion for the force-free case is the rotational kinetic energy:

$$T_{\text{rot}} = \frac{1}{2} \mathbf{L} \cdot \boldsymbol{\omega} = \text{constant}$$
*(11.140)*

But we have $\mathbf{L} = \text{constant}$, so $\boldsymbol{\omega}$ must move such that its projection on the stationary angular-momentum vector is constant. Thus, $\boldsymbol{\omega}$ precesses around and makes a constant angle with the vector $\mathbf{L}$. In such a case, $\mathbf{L}$, $\boldsymbol{\omega}$, and the $x_3$- (body) axis (i.e., the unit vector $\mathbf{e}_3$) all lie in a plane. We can show this by proving that $\mathbf{L} \cdot (\boldsymbol{\omega} \times \mathbf{e}_3) = 0$. First, $\boldsymbol{\omega} \times \mathbf{e}_3 = \omega_2 \mathbf{e}_1 - \omega_1 \mathbf{e}_2$. If we take the scalar product of this result with $\mathbf{L}$, we have $\mathbf{L} \cdot (\boldsymbol{\omega} \times \mathbf{e}_3) = L_1 \omega_2 - L_2 \omega_1 = I_1 \omega_1 \omega_2 - I_1 \omega_2 \omega_1 = 0$, because $I_1 = I_2$ for the symmetric top.

Therefore, if we designate the $x'_3$-axis in the fixed coordinate system to coincide with $\mathbf{L}$, then to an observer in the fixed system, $\boldsymbol{\omega}$ traces out a cone around the fixed $x'_3$-axis, called the space cone. The situation is then described (Figure 11-13) by one cone rolling on another, such that $\boldsymbol{\omega}$ precesses around the $x_3$-axis in the body system and around the $x'_3$-axis (along $\mathbf{L}$) in the space-fixed system.

![Figure 11-13](file:///c:/Users/Mester/Desktop/semester%204/AM%20II/exam/textbook/M-Chapter-11/41.png)
**Figure 11-13 Context:** We let the angular momentum $\mathbf{L}$ be along the fixed $x'_3$-axis. The angular velocity $\boldsymbol{\omega}$ traces out the body cone as it precesses about the $x_3$-axis in the body system, and it traces out the space cone as it precesses around the $x'_3$-axis in the space-fixed system. We can imagine the body cone rolling around the space cone.

The rate at which $\boldsymbol{\omega}$ precesses around the body symmetry axis is given by Equation 11.131:

$$\Omega = \frac{I_1 - I_3}{I_1} \omega_3$$

If $I_1 \approx I_3$, then $\Omega$ becomes very small compared with $\omega_3$. Earth is slightly flattened near the poles,[^16] so its shape can be approximated by an oblate spheroid with $I_1 = I_2$, but with $I_3 > I_1$. If Earth is considered to be a rigid body, then the moments $I_1$ and $I_3$ are such that $\Omega \approx \omega_3/300$. Because the period of Earth's rotation is $2\pi/\omega_3 = 1\text{ day}$, and because $\omega_3 \approx \omega$, the period predicted for the precession of the axis of rotation is $1/\Omega = 300\text{ days}$. The observed precession has an irregular period about 50 percent greater than that predicted on the basis of this simple theory; the deviation is ascribed to the facts that (1) Earth is not a rigid body and (2) the shape is not exactly that of an oblate spheroid, but rather has a higher-order deformation and actually resembles a flattened pear.

Earth's equatorial "bulge" together with the fact that Earth's rotational axis is inclined at an angle of approximately $23.5^\circ$ to the plane of Earth's orbit around the sun (the plane of the ecliptic) produces a gravitational torque (caused by both the Sun and the Moon), which produces a slow precession of Earth's axis. The period of this precessional motion is approximately 26,000 years. Thus, in different epochs, different stars become the "pole star."[^17]

**EXAMPLE 11.11** Show that the motion depicted in Figure 11-13 actually refers to the motion of a prolate object such as an elongated rod ($I_1 > I_3$), whereas for a flat disk ($I_3 > I_1$) the space cone would be inside the body cone rather than outside.

**Solution.** If $\mathbf{L}$ is along $x'_3$, then the Euler angle $\theta$ (between the $x'_3$- and $x_3$-axes) is the angle between $\mathbf{L}$ and the $x_3$-axis. At a given instant, we align $\boldsymbol{\omega}$ to be in the plane defined by $\mathbf{L}$, $\boldsymbol{\omega}$, and $\mathbf{e}_3$. Then, at this same instant:

$$\begin{aligned}
L_1 &= 0 \\
L_2 &= L \sin \theta \\
L_3 &= L \cos \theta
\end{aligned}$$
*(11.141)*

Let $\alpha$ be the angle between $\boldsymbol{\omega}$ and the $x_3$-axis. Then, at this same instant, we have:

$$\begin{aligned}
\omega_1 &= 0 \\
\omega_2 &= \omega \sin \alpha \\
\omega_3 &= \omega \cos \alpha
\end{aligned}$$
*(11.142)*

We can also determine the components of $\mathbf{L}$ from Equation 11.34:

$$\begin{aligned}
L_1 &= I_1 \omega_1 = 0 \\
L_2 &= I_1 \omega_2 = I_1 \omega \sin \alpha \\
L_3 &= I_3 \omega_3 = I_3 \omega \cos \alpha
\end{aligned}$$
*(11.143)*

We can obtain the ratio $L_2/L_3$ from Equations 11.141 and 11.143:

$$\frac{L_2}{L_3} = \tan \theta = \frac{I_1}{I_3} \tan \alpha$$
*(11.144)*

so we have:
- **Prolate spheroid ($I_1 > I_3$):** $\theta > \alpha$ *(11.145a)*
- **Oblate spheroid ($I_3 > I_1$):** $\alpha > \theta$ *(11.145b)*

![Figure 11-14](file:///c:/Users/Mester/Desktop/semester%204/AM%20II/exam/textbook/M-Chapter-11/43.png)
**Figure 11-14 Context:** Example 11.11. (a) When the body is prolate ($I_1 > I_3$), we have the situation here and in Figure 11-13. (b) When the body is oblate ($I_3 > I_1$), the inside of the body cone rotates around the outside of the space cone. The space cone is at rest in either case.

The two cases are shown in Figure 11-14. From Equation 11.131, we determine that $\Omega$ and $\omega_3$ have the same sign if $I_1 > I_3$, but have opposite signs if $I_3 > I_1$. Thus, the sense of precession is opposite for the two cases. This fact and Equation 11.145 can be reconciled only if the space cone is outside the body cone for the prolate case but inside the body cone for the oblate case. The angular velocity $\boldsymbol{\omega}$ defines both cones as it rotates about $\mathbf{L}$ (space cone) and the symmetry axis $x_3$ (body cone). The line of contact between the space and body cones is the instantaneous axis of rotation (along $\boldsymbol{\omega}$). At any instant, this axis is at rest, so that the body cone rolls around the space cone without slipping. In both cases, the space cone is fixed, because $\mathbf{L}$ is constant.

**EXAMPLE 11.12** With what angular velocity does the symmetry axis ($x_3$) and $\boldsymbol{\omega}$ rotate about the fixed angular momentum $\mathbf{L}$?

**Solution.** Because $\mathbf{e}_3$, $\boldsymbol{\omega}$, and $\mathbf{L}$ are in the same plane, $\mathbf{e}_3$ and $\boldsymbol{\omega}$ precess about $\mathbf{L}$ with the same angular velocity. In Section 11.8 we learned that $\dot{\phi}$ is the angular velocity along the $x'_3$-axis. If we use the same instant of time considered in the previous example (when $\mathbf{e}_1$ was perpendicular to the plane of $\mathbf{e}_3$, $\boldsymbol{\omega}$, and $\mathbf{L}$), then the Euler angle $\psi = 0$, and from Equation 11.102:

$$\omega_2 = \dot{\phi} \sin \theta$$

and:

$$\dot{\phi} = \frac{\omega_2}{\sin \theta}$$
*(11.146)*

Substituting for $\omega_2$ from Equation 11.142, we have:

$$\dot{\phi} = \omega \frac{\sin \alpha}{\sin \theta}$$
*(11.147)*

We can rewrite $\dot{\phi}$ by substituting $\sin \alpha$ from Equation 11.143 ($L \sin\theta = I_1 \omega \sin\alpha$):

$$\dot{\phi} = \frac{L}{I_1}$$
*(11.148)*

[^15]: In general, the constant coefficient is complex, so we should properly write $A \exp(i\delta)$. For simplicity, however, we set the phase $\delta$ equal to zero; this can always be done by choosing an appropriate instant to call $t = 0$.
[^16]: The flattening at the poles was shown by Newton to be caused by Earth's rotation; the resulting precessional motion was first calculated by Euler.
[^17]: This precession of the equinoxes was apparently discovered by the Babylonian astronomer Cidenas in about 348 B.C.
