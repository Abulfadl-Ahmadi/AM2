### Original Transcribed Notes

With respect to some point $O$ fixed in the body coordinate system, the angular momentum of the body is

$$\mathbf{L} = \sum_{\alpha} \mathbf{r}_\alpha \times \mathbf{p}_\alpha$$
*(11.16)*

The most convenient choice for the position of the point $O$ depends on the particular problem. Only two choices are important: (a) if one or more points of the body are fixed (in the fixed coordinate system), $O$ is chosen to coincide with one such point (as in the case of the rotating top, Section 11.11); (b) if no point of the body is fixed, $O$ is chosen to be the center of mass.

Relative to the body coordinate system, the linear momentum $\mathbf{p}_\alpha$ is:

$$\mathbf{p}_\alpha = m_\alpha \mathbf{v}_\alpha = m_\alpha \left( \boldsymbol{\omega} \times \mathbf{r}_\alpha \right)$$

Hence, the angular momentum of the body is

$$\mathbf{L} = \sum_{\alpha} m_\alpha \mathbf{r}_\alpha \times \left( \boldsymbol{\omega} \times \mathbf{r}_\alpha \right)$$
*(11.17)*

The vector identity $\mathbf{A} \times (\mathbf{B} \times \mathbf{A}) = A^2 \mathbf{B} - \mathbf{A}(\mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{B})$ can be used to express $\mathbf{L}$:

$$\mathbf{L} = \sum_{\alpha} m_\alpha \left[ r_\alpha^2 \boldsymbol{\omega} - \mathbf{r}_\alpha \left( \mathbf{r}_\alpha \cdot \boldsymbol{\omega} \right) \right]$$
*(11.18)*

The same technique we used to write $T_{\text{rot}}$ in tensor form can now be applied here. But the angular momentum is a vector, so for the $i$-th component, we write:

$$L_i = \sum_{\alpha} m_\alpha \left[ r_\alpha^2 \omega_i - r_{\alpha i} \sum_j r_{\alpha j} \omega_j \right]$$
$$L_i = \sum_j \omega_j \left[ \sum_{\alpha} m_\alpha \left( r_\alpha^2 \delta_{ij} - r_{\alpha i} r_{\alpha j} \right) \right]$$
*(11.19)*

The summation over $\alpha$ can be recognized (see Equation 11.10) as the $ij$-th element of the inertia tensor. Therefore,

$$L_i = \sum_j I_{ij} \omega_j$$
*(11.20a)*

or, in tensor notation,

$$\mathbf{L} = \{\mathbf{I}\} \cdot \boldsymbol{\omega}$$
*(11.20b)*

Thus, the inertia tensor relates a sum over the components of the angular velocity vector to the $i$-th component of the angular momentum vector. This may at first seem a somewhat unexpected result; for, if we consider a rigid body for which the inertia tensor has nonvanishing off-diagonal elements, then even if $\boldsymbol{\omega}$ is directed along, say, the $x_1$-direction, $\boldsymbol{\omega} = (\omega_1, 0, 0)$, the angular momentum vector in general has nonvanishing components in all three directions: $\mathbf{L} = (L_1, L_2, L_3)$; that is, the angular momentum vector does not in general have the same direction as the angular velocity vector. (It should be emphasized that this statement depends on $I_{ij} \neq 0$ for $i \neq j$; we return to this point in the next section.)

![Figure 11-4](file:///c:/Users/Mester/Desktop/semester%204/AM%20II/exam/textbook/M-Chapter-11/11.png)
**Figure 11-4 Context:** A dumbbell connected by masses $m_1$ and $m_2$ at the ends of its shaft. Note that $\boldsymbol{\omega}$ is not along the shaft, and that $\boldsymbol{\omega}$ and $\mathbf{L}$ are not collinear.

As an example of $\boldsymbol{\omega}$ and $\mathbf{L}$ not being collinear, consider the rotating dumbbell in Figure 11-4. (We consider the shaft connecting $m_1$ and $m_2$ to be weightless and extensionless.) The relation connecting $\mathbf{v}_\alpha$, $\mathbf{r}_\alpha$, and $\boldsymbol{\omega}$ is:

$$\mathbf{v}_\alpha = \boldsymbol{\omega} \times \mathbf{r}_\alpha$$

and the relation connecting $\mathbf{r}_\alpha$, $\mathbf{v}_\alpha$, and $\mathbf{L}$ is:

$$\mathbf{L} = \sum_{\alpha} m_\alpha \mathbf{r}_\alpha \times \mathbf{v}_\alpha$$

It should be clear that $\boldsymbol{\omega}$ is directed along the axis of rotation and that $\mathbf{L}$ is perpendicular to the line connecting $m_1$ and $m_2$.

We note, for this example, that the angular momentum vector $\mathbf{L}$ does not remain constant in time but rotates with an angular velocity $\boldsymbol{\omega}$ in such a way that it traces out a cone whose axis is the axis of rotation. Therefore, $\dot{\mathbf{L}} \neq 0$. But Equation 9.31 states that:

$$\dot{\mathbf{L}} = \mathbf{N}$$
*(11.21)*

where $\mathbf{N}$ is the external torque applied to the body. Thus, to keep the dumbbell rotating as in Figure 11-4, we must constantly apply a torque.

We can obtain another result from Equation 11.20a by multiplying $L_i$ by $\omega_i$ and summing over $i$:

$$\sum_i L_i \omega_i = \sum_{i,j} I_{ij} \omega_i \omega_j = 2 T_{\text{rot}}$$
*(11.22a)*

where the second equality is just Equation 11.11. Thus,

$$T_{\text{rot}} = \frac{1}{2} \mathbf{L} \cdot \boldsymbol{\omega}$$
*(11.22b)*

Equations 11.20b and 11.22b illustrate two important properties of tensors:
- The product of a tensor and a vector yields a vector, as in:
  $$\mathbf{L} = \{\mathbf{I}\} \cdot \boldsymbol{\omega}$$
- The product of a tensor and two vectors yields a scalar, as in:
  $$T_{\text{rot}} = \frac{1}{2} \boldsymbol{\omega} \cdot \{\mathbf{I}\} \cdot \boldsymbol{\omega}$$

We shall not, however, have occasion to use tensor equations in this form. We use only the summation (or integral) expressions as in Equations 11.11, 11.15, and 11.20a.

**EXAMPLE 11.4** Consider the pendulum shown in Figure 11-5 composed of a rigid rod of length $b$ with a mass $m_1$ at its end. Another mass ($m_2$) is placed halfway down the rod. Find the frequency of small oscillations if the pendulum swings in a plane.

![Figure 11-5](file:///c:/Users/Mester/Desktop/semester%204/AM%20II/exam/textbook/M-Chapter-11/12.png)
**Figure 11-5 Context:** Example 11.4. A rigid rod rotating as a pendulum has a mass $m_1$ at its end and another mass $m_2$ halfway.

**Solution.** We use the methods of this chapter to analyze the system. Let the fixed and body systems have their origin at the pendulum pivot point. Let $\mathbf{e}_1$ be along the rod, $\mathbf{e}_2$ be in the plane of rotation, and $\mathbf{e}_3$ be out of the plane (Figure 11-5). The angular velocity is

$$\boldsymbol{\omega} = \dot{\theta} \mathbf{e}_3$$
*(11.23)*

We use Equation 11.10 to find the inertia tensor. All the mass is along $\mathbf{e}_1$, with $x_{11} = b$ and $x_{21} = b/2$. All other components equal zero:

$$I_{ij} = m_1 \left( \delta_{ij} r_1^2 - r_{1i} r_{1j} \right) + m_2 \left( \delta_{ij} r_2^2 - r_{2i} r_{2j} \right)$$
*(11.24)*

The inertia tensor, Equation 11.13a, becomes:

$$\{\mathbf{I}\} = \begin{pmatrix}
0 & 0 & 0 \\
0 & m_1 b^2 + m_2 (b/2)^2 & 0 \\
0 & 0 & m_1 b^2 + m_2 (b/2)^2
\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}
0 & 0 & 0 \\
0 & \left( m_1 + \frac{m_2}{4} \right) b^2 & 0 \\
0 & 0 & \left( m_1 + \frac{m_2}{4} \right) b^2
\end{pmatrix}$$
*(11.25)*

We determine the angular momentum from Equation 11.20a:

$$L_i = \sum_j I_{ij} \omega_j$$
$$L_3 = I_{33} \omega_3 = \left( m_1 + \frac{m_2}{4} \right) b^2 \dot{\theta}$$
*(11.26)*

with $L_1 = L_2 = 0$.

The only external force is gravity, which causes a torque $\mathbf{N}$ on the system. Because $\dot{\mathbf{L}} = \mathbf{N}$, we have:

$$\frac{d}{dt} \left[ \left( m_1 + \frac{m_2}{4} \right) b^2 \dot{\theta} \right] \mathbf{e}_3 = \sum_\alpha \mathbf{r}_\alpha \times \mathbf{F}_{g\alpha}$$
*(11.27)*

Because the gravitational force is down,

$$\mathbf{g} = g \cos \theta \mathbf{e}_1 - g \sin \theta \mathbf{e}_2$$

Thus,

$$\mathbf{r}_1 \times \mathbf{F}_{g1} = (b \mathbf{e}_1) \times \left( m_1 g \cos \theta \mathbf{e}_1 - m_1 g \sin \theta \mathbf{e}_2 \right) = -m_1 gb \sin \theta \mathbf{e}_3$$
$$\mathbf{r}_2 \times \mathbf{F}_{g2} = \left( \frac{b}{2} \mathbf{e}_1 \right) \times \left( m_2 g \cos \theta \mathbf{e}_1 - m_2 g \sin \theta \mathbf{e}_2 \right) = -m_2 g \frac{b}{2} \sin \theta \mathbf{e}_3$$

Equation 11.27 becomes:

$$\left( m_1 + \frac{m_2}{4} \right) b^2 \ddot{\theta} = -gb \sin \theta \left( m_1 + \frac{m_2}{2} \right)$$
*(11.28)*

and the frequency of small oscillations is:

$$\omega_0^2 = \frac{m_1 + m_2/2}{m_1 + m_2/4} \frac{g}{b}$$
*(11.29)*

We can check Equation 11.29 by noting that $\omega_0^2 \to g/b$ for $m_1 \gg m_2$, and $\omega_0^2 \to 2g/b$ for $m_2 \gg m_1$, as it should.

This example could have just as easily been solved by finding the kinetic energy from Equation 11.22a and using Lagrange's equations of motion. We would then have:

$$T_{\text{rot}} = \frac{1}{2} L_3 \omega_3 = \frac{1}{2} \left( m_1 + \frac{m_2}{4} \right) b^2 \dot{\theta}^2$$
*(11.30)*

$$U = -m_1 gb \cos \theta - m_2 g \frac{b}{2} \cos \theta$$
*(11.31)*

where $U = 0$ at the origin. The equation of motion (Equation 11.28) follows directly from a straightforward application of the Lagrangian technique.
