## 11.6 Moments of Inertia for Different Body Coordinate Systems

### Original Transcribed Notes

For the kinetic energy to be separable into translational and rotational portions (see Equation 11.6), it is, in general, necessary to choose a body coordinate system whose origin is the center of mass of the body. For certain geometrical shapes, however, the calculation of the inertia tensor elements with respect to a center-of-mass origin is more difficult than with respect to some other origin. We show here how the tensor elements calculated for one origin can be related to those for a parallel coordinate system with its origin at the center of mass.

Consider two body coordinate systems:
- System $\{x_i\}$ with origin $O$ at the center of mass of the body.
- System $\{X_i\}$ with origin $Q$ not located at the center of mass, but whose axes are parallel to the $x_i$-axes (Figure 11-6).

![Figure 11-6](file:///c:/Users/Mester/Desktop/semester%204/AM%20II/exam/textbook/M-Chapter-11/19.png)
**Figure 11-6 Context:** The coordinate axes $X_i$ are fixed in the body and have the same orientation as the $x_i$-axes, but its origin $Q$ is not located at the origin $O$ (at the center of mass of the body system).

If the vector connecting $Q$ with $O$ is $\mathbf{a}$, then the general position vector $\mathbf{R}_\alpha$ in the $\{X_i\}$ system (Figure 11-6) can be written as:

$$\mathbf{R}_\alpha = \mathbf{a} + \mathbf{r}_\alpha$$
*(11.44)*

with components:

$$X_{\alpha i} = a_i + r_{\alpha i}$$
*(11.45)*

Using Equation 11.45, the tensor element $J_{ij}$ (referred to the origin $Q$) becomes:

$$J_{ij} = \sum_{\alpha} m_\alpha \left[ \left( \sum_k X_{\alpha k}^2 \right) \delta_{ij} - X_{\alpha i} X_{\alpha j} \right]$$
$$J_{ij} = \sum_{\alpha} m_\alpha \left[ \sum_k (r_{\alpha k} + a_k)^2 \delta_{ij} - (r_{\alpha i} + a_i)(r_{\alpha j} + a_j) \right]$$
$$J_{ij} = \sum_{\alpha} m_\alpha \left[ \left( r_\alpha^2 + 2\mathbf{r}_\alpha \cdot \mathbf{a} + a^2 \right) \delta_{ij} - (r_{\alpha i} r_{\alpha j} + r_{\alpha i} a_j + r_{\alpha j} a_i + a_i a_j) \right]$$
*(11.46)*

Identifying the first summation as $I_{ij}$ (referred to the center-of-mass origin $O$), we have, on regrouping:

$$J_{ij} = I_{ij} + \sum_{\alpha} m_\alpha (a^2 \delta_{ij} - a_i a_j) + \sum_{\alpha} m_\alpha \left( 2\mathbf{r}_\alpha \cdot \mathbf{a} \delta_{ij} - r_{\alpha i} a_j - r_{\alpha j} a_i \right)$$
*(11.47)*

But each term in the last summation involves a sum of the form:

$$\sum_{\alpha} m_\alpha r_{\alpha k}$$

We know, however, that because $O$ is located at the center of mass,

$$\sum_{\alpha} m_\alpha \mathbf{r}_\alpha = 0$$

or, for the $k$th component,

$$\sum_{\alpha} m_\alpha r_{\alpha k} = 0$$

Therefore, all such terms in Equation 11.47 vanish, and we have:

$$J_{ij} = I_{ij} + M(a^2 \delta_{ij} - a_i a_j)$$
*(11.48)*

where we have used $\sum_\alpha m_\alpha = M$. Solving for $I_{ij}$, we have the result:

$$I_{ij} = J_{ij} - M(a^2 \delta_{ij} - a_i a_j)$$
*(11.49)*

which allows the calculation of the elements $I_{ij}$ of the desired inertia tensor (with origin at the center of mass) once those with respect to the $X_i$-axes are known. The second term on the right-hand side of Equation 11.49 is the inertia tensor referred to the origin $Q$ for a point mass $M$ located at $O$.

Equation 11.49 is the general form of Steiner's parallel-axis theorem.[^6] Consider, for example, Figure 11-7. The diagonal element $I_{11}$ is:

$$I_{11} = J_{11} - M(a^2 \delta_{11} - a_1^2) = J_{11} - M(a^2 - a_1^2)$$
$$I_{11} = J_{11} - M(a_2^2 + a_3^2)$$

![Figure 11-7](file:///c:/Users/Mester/Desktop/semester%204/AM%20II/exam/textbook/M-Chapter-11/20.png)
**Figure 11-7 Context:** The elements $I_{ij}$ in the $x_i$-axes are related to those ($J_{ij}$) in the $X_i$-axes by Equation 11.49. The vector $\mathbf{a}$ connects the origin $Q$ with the origin $O$.

which states that the difference between the elements is equal to the mass of the body multiplied by the square of the distance between the parallel axes (in this case, the distance between the $x_1$- and $X_1$-axes).

---

**EXAMPLE 11.6** Find the inertia tensor of the cube of Example 11.3 in a coordinate system with its origin at the center of mass.

**Solution.** In Example 11.3, with the origin at the corner of the cube, we found the inertia tensor $\{\mathbf{J}\}$ to be:

$$\{\mathbf{J}\} = Mb^2 \begin{pmatrix} 2/3 & -1/4 & -1/4 \\ -1/4 & 2/3 & -1/4 \\ -1/4 & -1/4 & 2/3 \end{pmatrix}$$
*(11.50)*

We may now use Equation 11.49 to obtain the inertia tensor $\{\mathbf{I}\}$ referred to a coordinate system with its origin at the center of mass. In keeping with the notation of this section, we call the new axes $x_i$ with origin $O$ and call the previous axes $X_i$ with origin $Q$ at one corner of the cube (Figure 11-8).

The center of mass of the cube is at the point $(b/2, b/2, b/2)$ in the $X_i$ coordinate system, and the components of the vector $\mathbf{a}$ therefore are:

$$a_1 = a_2 = a_3 = \frac{b}{2}$$

From Equation 11.50, we have:

$$J_{11} = J_{22} = J_{33} = \frac{2}{3}Mb^2$$
$$J_{12} = J_{21} = J_{13} = J_{31} = J_{23} = J_{32} = -\frac{1}{4}Mb^2$$

![Figure 11-8](file:///c:/Users/Mester/Desktop/semester%204/AM%20II/exam/textbook/M-Chapter-11/21.png)
**Figure 11-8 Context:** Example 11.6. The $X_i$-axes have their origin $Q$ at one corner of a cube of sides $b$. The system $x_i$ has its origin $O$ at the cube's center of mass.

Applying Equation 11.49, we find:

$$I_{11} = J_{11} - M(a^2 - a_1^2)$$

Since $a^2 = a_1^2 + a_2^2 + a_3^2 = 3(b^2/4) = \frac{3}{4}b^2$, we have $a^2 - a_1^2 = \frac{3}{4}b^2 - \frac{1}{4}b^2 = \frac{1}{2}b^2$. Thus,

$$I_{11} = \frac{2}{3}Mb^2 - M\left(\frac{1}{2}b^2\right) = \frac{1}{6}Mb^2$$

and:

$$I_{12} = J_{12} - M(-a_1 a_2) = -\frac{1}{4}Mb^2 + M\left(\frac{b^2}{4}\right) = 0$$

Altogether, we have:

$$I_{11} = I_{22} = I_{33} = \frac{1}{6}Mb^2$$
$$I_{12} = I_{21} = I_{13} = I_{31} = I_{23} = I_{32} = 0$$

The inertia tensor is therefore diagonal:

$$\{\mathbf{I}\} = \begin{pmatrix} \frac{1}{6}Mb^2 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & \frac{1}{6}Mb^2 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & \frac{1}{6}Mb^2 \end{pmatrix}$$
*(11.51)*

If we factor out the common term $\frac{1}{6}Mb^2$ from this expression, we can write:

$$\{\mathbf{I}\} = \frac{1}{6}Mb^2 \{\mathbf{1}\}$$
*(11.52)*

where $\{\mathbf{1}\}$ is the unit tensor:

$$\{\mathbf{1}\} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$$
*(11.53)*

Thus, we find that, for the choice of the origin at the center of mass of the cube, the principal axes are perpendicular to the faces of the cube. Because, from a physical standpoint, nothing distinguishes any one of these axes from another, the principal moments of inertia are all equal for this case. We note further that, as long as we maintain the origin at the center of mass, then the inertia tensor is the same for any orientation of the coordinate axes and these axes are equally valid principal axes.[^7]

[^6]: Jacob Steiner (1796–1863).
[^7]: In this regard, the cube is similar to a sphere as far as the inertia tensor is concerned (i.e., for an origin at the center of mass, the structure of the inertia tensor elements is not sufficiently detailed to discriminate between a cube and a sphere).
