## 9.2 Center of Mass

### Original Transcribed Notes

Consider a system composed of $n$ particles, with each particle's mass described by $m_{\alpha}$, where $\alpha$ is an index from $\alpha=1$ to $\alpha=n$. The total mass of the system is denoted by $M$,

$$M=\sum_{\alpha}m_{\alpha}$$

where the summation over $\alpha$ (as in all summations carried out over Greek indices) runs from $\alpha=1$ to $\alpha=n$. Such a system is displayed in Figure 9-2. If the vector connecting the origin with the $\alpha$th particle is $r_{\alpha}$, then the vector defining the position of the system's center of mass is

$$R=\frac{1}{M}\sum_{\alpha}m_{\alpha}r_{\alpha}$$

For a continuous distribution of mass, the summation is replaced by an integral,

$$R=\frac{1}{M}\int r~dm$$

The location of the center of mass of a body is uniquely defined, but the position vector $R$ depends on the coordinate system chosen. If the origin in Figure 9-2 were chosen elsewhere, the vector $R$ would be different.

**EXAMPLE 9.1** Find the center of mass of a solid hemisphere of constant density.

**Solution.** Let the density be $\rho$, the hemispherical mass be $M$, and the radius be $a$.

$$\rho=\frac{M}{\frac{2}{3}\pi a^{3}}$$

_(Note: Corrected from an apparent OCR typo in the original text which read $a^9$)_

We want to choose the origin of our coordinate system carefully (Figure 9-3) to make the problem as simple as possible. The position coordinates of $R$ are $(X, Y, Z)$. From symmetry, $X=0$, $Z=0$. This should be obvious from Equation 9.4,

$$X=\frac{1}{M}\int_{-a}^{a}x~dm$$

$$Z=\frac{1}{M}\int_{-a}^{a}z~dm$$

because we are integrating over an odd power of a variable with symmetric limits. For $Y$, however, the limits are asymmetric.

$$Y=\frac{1}{M}\int_{0}^{a}y~dm$$

Construct $dm$ so it is placed at a constant value of $y$. A circular slice perpendicular to the $y$-axis suffices (see Figure 9-3).

$$dm=\rho dV=\rho\pi(a^{2}-y^{2})dy$$

$$Y=\frac{1}{M}\int_{0}^{a}\rho\pi y(a^{2}-y^{2})dy$$

$$Y=\frac{\pi\rho a^{4}}{4M}=\frac{3a}{8}$$

The position of the center of mass is $(0, 3a/8, 0)$.
