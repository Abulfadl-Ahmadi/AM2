**EXAMPLE 11.1** A string attached to the ceiling is wrapped around a homogeneous cylinder of mass $M$ and radius $R$ (see Figure 11-1). At time $t = 0$, the cylinder is dropped from rest and rotates as the string unwinds. Find the tension $T$ in the string, the linear and angular accelerations of the cylinder, and the angular velocity about the cylinder's center.

![Figure 11-1](file:///c:/Users/Mester/Desktop/semester%204/AM%20II/exam/textbook/M-Chapter-11/03.png)
**Figure 11-1 Context:** Example 11.1. A string attached to the ceiling is wrapped around a cylinder. The cylinder is released from rest.

**Solution.** The center of mass moves due to the sum of the forces, which are all in the vertical direction. We let $y$ point downward:

$$M\ddot{y} = F_g - T = Mg - T$$
*(11.1)*

where the center of mass acceleration is $\ddot{y}$, and we have used $F_g = Mg$. The rotation about the cylinder's center of mass at $O$ is due to the tension $T$:

$$\tau = RT = I\dot{\omega} = I\ddot{\theta}$$
*(11.2)*

where $\tau$ is the torque about $O$, and $I$ is the rotational inertia of the cylinder ($MR^2/2$). We let $y = 0$ and $\theta = 0$ at $t = 0$ when the cylinder is released. Then we have $y = R\theta$, $v = \dot{y} = R\dot{\theta} = R\omega$, and $a = \ddot{y} = R\ddot{\theta} = R\dot{\omega}$. We can combine these relations with Equations 11.1 and 11.2 to determine the acceleration:

$$Mg - T = M(R\dot{\omega}) = M\left(\frac{RT}{I}\right)R = \frac{MR^2 T}{MR^2/2} = 2T$$

which gives $T = Mg/3$. Substituting this back into Equation 11.1, we obtain the linear acceleration:

$$\ddot{y} = \frac{2}{3}g$$

and the angular acceleration:

$$\alpha = \ddot{\theta} = \frac{\ddot{y}}{R} = \frac{2g}{3R}$$

The tension $T$ is:

$$T = \frac{1}{3}Mg$$
*(11.3)*

The angular velocity is $\omega = \dot{\theta} = \int \ddot{\theta}\,dt$. We integrate $\ddot{y}$ to obtain $v = \dot{y} = \frac{2}{3}gt$ and $\omega = \frac{2}{3}\frac{gt}{R}$.

**EXAMPLE 11.2** A physical or compound pendulum is a rigid body that oscillates due to its own weight about a horizontal axis that does not pass through the center of mass of the body (Figure 11-2). For small oscillations, find the frequency and period of oscillation if the mass of the body is $M$ and the radius of gyration is $k$.

![Figure 11-2](file:///c:/Users/Mester/Desktop/semester%204/AM%20II/exam/textbook/M-Chapter-11/04.png)
**Figure 11-2 Context:** Example 11.2. The physical or compound pendulum. The body rotates about an axis passing through $O$. The body rotates due to the gravitational force acting at the center of mass.

**Solution.** We use the Lagrangian method to solve this example, although we could just as easily solve for the torque to find the equation of motion. The rotation axis passes through the point $O$ of the body. Let $d$ be the distance from the pivot point $O$ to the center of mass. The radius of gyration $k$ is defined such that the rotational inertia $I$ about the given axis of rotation ($O$ in this case) is given by $I = Mk^2$.

The kinetic energy of rotation and the potential energy are:

$$T = \frac{1}{2} I \dot{\theta}^2$$
$$U = -Mgd \cos \theta \approx -Mgd \left( 1 - \frac{\theta^2}{2} \right)$$

where we have defined the zero of the potential energy to be at point $O$ and have used the small-angle approximation for $\cos \theta$. We find the Lagrangian function and take the appropriate derivatives to form the Lagrange equation of motion. The generalized coordinate is clearly $\theta$:

$$L = T - U \approx \frac{1}{2} I \dot{\theta}^2 + Mgd \left( 1 - \frac{\theta^2}{2} \right)$$

The Lagrange equation of motion is:

$$\frac{d}{dt}\left(\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{\theta}}\right) - \frac{\partial L}{\partial \theta} = 0$$

$$I\ddot{\theta} + Mgd\theta = 0 \implies \ddot{\theta} + \frac{Mgd}{I}\theta = 0$$

We have seen this equation several times, and the angular frequency is given by $\omega^2 = Mgd/I$. From this we find the frequency $\nu$ and period $\tau$:

$$\nu = \frac{\omega}{2\pi} = \frac{1}{2\pi}\sqrt{\frac{Mgd}{I}} = \frac{1}{2\pi}\sqrt{\frac{gd}{k^2}}$$
$$\tau = \frac{2\pi}{\omega} = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{I}{Mgd}} = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{k^2}{gd}}$$
