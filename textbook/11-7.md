## 11.7 Further Properties of the Inertia Tensor

### Original Transcribed Notes

Before attacking the problems of rigid-body dynamics by obtaining the general equations of motion, we should consider the fundamental importance of some of the operations we have been discussing. Let us begin by examining the properties of the inertia tensor under coordinate transformations.[^8]

We have already obtained the fundamental relation connecting the inertia tensor and the angular momentum and angular velocity vectors (Equation 11.20), which we can write as:

$$L_i = \sum_j I_{ij} \omega_j$$
*(11.54a)*

Because this is a vector equation, in a coordinate system rotated with respect to the system for which Equation 11.54a applies, we must have an entirely analogous relation:

$$L'_i = \sum_j I'_{ij} \omega'_j$$
*(11.54b)*

where the primed quantities all refer to the rotated system. Both $\mathbf{L}$ and $\boldsymbol{\omega}$ obey the standard transformation equation for vectors:

$$L'_i = \sum_j \lambda_{ij} L_j$$
*(11.55a)*

and:

$$\omega'_i = \sum_j \lambda_{ij} \omega_j \implies \omega_j = \sum_k \lambda_{kj} \omega'_k$$
*(11.55b)*

where we have used the orthogonality of the transformation matrix $\boldsymbol{\lambda}$. If we substitute Equations 11.55a and 11.55b into Equation 11.54a, we obtain:

$$\sum_j \lambda_{ji} L'_j = \sum_j I_{ij} \sum_k \lambda_{kj} \omega'_k$$
*(11.56)*

Next, we multiply both sides of this equation by $\lambda_{lm}$ and sum over $i$:

$$\sum_j \left( \sum_i \lambda_{lm} \lambda_{ji} \right) L'_j = \sum_k \left( \sum_{i,j} \lambda_{lm} \lambda_{kj} I_{ij} \right) \omega'_k$$
*(11.57)*

The term in parentheses on the left-hand side is just $\delta_{mj}$ (since $\boldsymbol{\lambda}$ is orthogonal), so performing the summation over $j$, we obtain:

$$L'_m = \sum_k \left( \sum_{i,j} \lambda_{li} \lambda_{kj} I_{ij} \right) \omega'_k$$
*(11.58)*

For this equation to be identical with Equation 11.54b, we must have:

$$I'_{mk} = \sum_{i,j} \lambda_{mi} \lambda_{kj} I_{ij}$$
*(11.59)*

This is the rule that the inertia tensor must obey under a coordinate transformation. Equation 11.59 is, in fact, the general rule specifying the manner in which any second-rank tensor must transform. For a tensor $\{\mathbf{T}\}$ of arbitrary rank, the transformation rule is:

$$T'_{ijk\dots} = \sum_{p,q,r,\dots} \lambda_{ip} \lambda_{jq} \lambda_{kr} \dots T_{pqr\dots}$$
*(11.60)*

Note that we can write Equation 11.59 as:

$$I'_{ij} = \sum_{k,l} \lambda_{ik} \lambda_{jl} I_{kl}$$
*(11.61)*

Although matrices and tensors are distinct types of mathematical objects, the manipulation of tensors is in many respects the same as for matrices. Thus, Equation 11.61 can be expressed as a matrix equation:

$$\mathbf{I}' = \boldsymbol{\lambda} \mathbf{I} \boldsymbol{\lambda}^T$$
*(11.62)*

where we understand $\mathbf{I}$ to be the matrix consisting of the elements of the tensor $\{\mathbf{I}\}$. Because we are considering only orthogonal transformation matrices, the transpose of $\boldsymbol{\lambda}$ is equal to its inverse ($\boldsymbol{\lambda}^T = \boldsymbol{\lambda}^{-1}$), so we can express Equation 11.62 as:

$$\mathbf{I}' = \boldsymbol{\lambda} \mathbf{I} \boldsymbol{\lambda}^{-1}$$
*(11.63)*

A transformation of this general type is called a similarity transformation.

**EXAMPLE 11.7** Prove the assertion stated in Example 11.6 that the inertia tensor for a cube (with origin at the center of mass) is independent of the orientation of the axes.

**Solution.** The change in the inertia tensor under a rotation of the coordinate axes can be computed by making a similarity transformation. Thus, if the rotation is described by the matrix $\boldsymbol{\lambda}$, we have:

$$\mathbf{I}' = \boldsymbol{\lambda} \mathbf{I} \boldsymbol{\lambda}^{-1}$$
*(11.64)*

But the matrix $\mathbf{I}$, which is derived from the elements of the tensor $\{\mathbf{I}\}$ (Equation 11.52 of Example 11.6), is just the identity matrix $\mathbf{1}$ multiplied by a constant:

$$\mathbf{I} = \frac{1}{6} Mb^2 \mathbf{1} = \frac{1}{6} Mb^2 \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$$
*(11.65)*

Therefore, the operations specified in Equation 11.64 are trivial:

$$\mathbf{I}' = \boldsymbol{\lambda} \left( \frac{1}{6} Mb^2 \mathbf{1} \right) \boldsymbol{\lambda}^{-1} = \frac{1}{6} Mb^2 \boldsymbol{\lambda} \boldsymbol{\lambda}^{-1} = \frac{1}{6} Mb^2 \mathbf{1} = \mathbf{I}$$
*(11.66)*

Thus, the transformed inertia tensor is identical to the original tensor, independent of the details of the rotation.

---

Let us next determine what condition must be satisfied if we take an arbitrary inertia tensor and perform a coordinate rotation in such a way that the transformed inertia tensor is diagonal. Such an operation implies that the quantity $I'_{ij}$ in Equation 11.59 must satisfy (see Equation 11.32) the relation:

$$I'_{ij} = I_i \delta_{ij}$$
*(11.67)*

Thus,

$$I_i \delta_{ij} = \sum_{k,l} \lambda_{ik} \lambda_{jl} I_{kl}$$
*(11.68)*

If we multiply both sides of this equation by $\lambda_{im}$ and sum over $i$, we obtain:

$$\sum_i \lambda_{im} I_i \delta_{ij} = \sum_l \left( \sum_i \lambda_{im} \lambda_{ik} \right) \lambda_{jl} I_{kl}$$
*(11.69)*

The term in parentheses on the right-hand side is just $\delta_{mk}$, so the summation over $i$ on the left-hand side of the equation and the summation over $k$ on the right-hand side yield:

$$\lambda_{jm} I_j = \sum_l \lambda_{jl} I_{ml}$$
*(11.70)*

Now, since the tensor is symmetric ($I_{ml} = I_{lm}$), the right-hand side of this equation can be written as:

$$\sum_l I_{ml} \lambda_{jl} = I_j \lambda_{jm}$$
*(11.71)*

so Equation 11.70 becomes:

$$\sum_l I_{ml} \lambda_{jl} = I_j \lambda_{jm}$$
*(11.72a)*

or:

$$\sum_l (I_{ml} - I_j \delta_{ml}) \lambda_{jl} = 0$$
*(11.72b)*

This is a set of simultaneous linear algebraic equations; for each value of $j$ there are three such equations, one for each of the three possible values of $m$. For a nontrivial solution to exist, the determinant of the coefficients must vanish, so the principal moments of inertia $I_1$, $I_2$, and $I_3$ are obtained as roots of the secular determinant:

$$|I_{ml} - I \delta_{ml}| = 0$$
*(11.73)*

This equation is just Equation 11.39; it is a cubic equation that yields the principal moments of inertia.

Thus, for any inertia tensor, the elements of which are computed for a given origin, it is possible to perform a rotation of the coordinate axes about that origin in such a way that the inertia tensor becomes diagonal. The new coordinate axes are then the principal axes of the body, and the new moments are the principal moments of inertia. Thus, for any body and for any choice of origin, there always exists a set of principal axes.

**EXAMPLE 11.8** For the cube of Example 11.3, diagonalize the inertia tensor by rotating the coordinate axes.

**Solution.** We choose the origin to lie at one corner and perform the rotation in such a manner that the $x_3'$-axis is rotated to the original diagonal of the cube. Such a rotation can conveniently be made in two steps:
1. Rotate through an angle of $45^\circ$ about the $x_3$-axis.
2. Rotate through an angle of $\theta = \cos^{-1}(\sqrt{2/3}) \approx 35.26^\circ$ about the new $x_2'$-axis.

The first rotation matrix is:

$$\boldsymbol{\lambda}_1 = \begin{pmatrix} \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} & \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} & 0 \\ -\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} & \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$$
*(11.74)*

and the second rotation matrix is:

$$\boldsymbol{\lambda}_2 = \begin{pmatrix} \sqrt{\frac{2}{3}} & 0 & -\frac{1}{\sqrt{3}} \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\ \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}} & 0 & \sqrt{\frac{2}{3}} \end{pmatrix}$$
*(11.75)*

The combined rotation matrix $\boldsymbol{\lambda} = \boldsymbol{\lambda}_2 \boldsymbol{\lambda}_1$ is:

$$\boldsymbol{\lambda} = \begin{pmatrix} \frac{1}{\sqrt{6}} & \frac{1}{\sqrt{6}} & -\sqrt{\frac{2}{3}} \\ -\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} & \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} & 0 \\ \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}} & \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}} & \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}} \end{pmatrix}$$
*(11.76)*

The matrix form of the transformed inertia tensor is:

$$\mathbf{I}' = \boldsymbol{\lambda} \mathbf{I} \boldsymbol{\lambda}^T$$
*(11.77)*

$$\mathbf{I}' = \beta \begin{pmatrix} \frac{1}{\sqrt{6}} & \frac{1}{\sqrt{6}} & -\sqrt{\frac{2}{3}} \\ -\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} & \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} & 0 \\ \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}} & \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}} & \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}} \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 2/3 & -1/4 & -1/4 \\ -1/4 & 2/3 & -1/4 \\ -1/4 & -1/4 & 2/3 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} \frac{1}{\sqrt{6}} & -\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} & \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}} \\ \frac{1}{\sqrt{6}} & \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} & \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}} \\ -\sqrt{\frac{2}{3}} & 0 & \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}} \end{pmatrix}$$
$$\mathbf{I}' = \beta \begin{pmatrix} 11/12 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 11/12 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1/6 \end{pmatrix}$$
*(11.78)*

Equation 11.78 is just the matrix form of the inertia tensor found by the diagonalization procedure using the secular determinant (Equation 11.41 of Example 11.5).

Next, we seek to prove that the principal axes actually form an orthogonal set. Let us assume that we have solved the secular equation and have determined the principal moments of inertia, all of which are distinct. We know that for each principal moment there exists a corresponding principal axis with the property that, if the angular velocity vector $\boldsymbol{\omega}$ lies along this axis, then the angular momentum vector $\mathbf{L}$ is similarly oriented; that is, to each $I_m$ there corresponds an angular velocity $\boldsymbol{\omega}_m$ with components $\omega_{1m}$, $\omega_{2m}$, $\omega_{3m}$. For the $m$-th principal moment, we have:

$$L_{im} = I_m \omega_{im}$$
*(11.79)*

In terms of the elements of the moment-of-inertia tensor, we also have:

$$L_{im} = \sum_k I_{ik} \omega_{km}$$
*(11.80)*

Combining these two relations, we have:

$$\sum_k I_{ik} \omega_{km} = I_m \omega_{im}$$
*(11.81a)*

Similarly, we can write for the $n$-th principal moment:

$$\sum_i I_{ki} \omega_{in} = I_n \omega_{kn}$$
*(11.81b)*

If we multiply Equation 11.81a by $\omega_{in}$ and sum over $i$, and then multiply Equation 11.81b by $\omega_{km}$ and sum over $k$, we have:

$$\sum_{i,k} I_{ik} \omega_{km} \omega_{in} = I_m \sum_i \omega_{im} \omega_{in}$$
$$\sum_{i,k} I_{ki} \omega_{in} \omega_{km} = I_n \sum_k \omega_{kn} \omega_{km}$$
*(11.82)*

The left-hand sides of these equations are identical, because the inertia tensor is symmetrical ($I_{ik} = I_{ki}$). Therefore, on subtracting the second equation from the first, we have:

$$I_m \sum_i \omega_{im} \omega_{in} - I_n \sum_k \omega_{km} \omega_{kn} = 0$$
*(11.83)*

Because $i$ and $k$ are both dummy indices, we can replace them by $j$, say, and obtain:

$$(I_m - I_n) \sum_j \omega_{jm} \omega_{jn} = 0$$
*(11.84)*

By hypothesis, the principal moments are distinct, so that $I_m \neq I_n$. Therefore, Equation 11.84 can be satisfied only if:

$$\sum_j \omega_{jm} \omega_{jn} = 0$$
*(11.85)*

But this summation is just the definition of the scalar product of the vectors $\boldsymbol{\omega}_m$ and $\boldsymbol{\omega}_n$. Hence,

$$\boldsymbol{\omega}_m \cdot \boldsymbol{\omega}_n = 0$$
*(11.86)*

Because the principal moments $I_m$ and $I_n$ were picked arbitrarily from the set of three moments, we conclude that each pair of principal axes is perpendicular; the three principal axes therefore constitute an orthogonal set.

If a double root of the secular equation exists, so that the principal moments are $I_1 = I_2$, then the preceding analysis shows that the angular velocity vectors satisfy the relations:

$$\boldsymbol{\omega}_1 \cdot \boldsymbol{\omega}_3 = 0, \quad \boldsymbol{\omega}_2 \cdot \boldsymbol{\omega}_3 = 0$$

but that nothing may be said regarding the angle between $\boldsymbol{\omega}_1$ and $\boldsymbol{\omega}_2$. But the fact that $I_1 = I_2$ implies that the body possesses an axis of symmetry. Therefore, $\boldsymbol{\omega}_3$ lies along the symmetry axis, and $\boldsymbol{\omega}_1$ and $\boldsymbol{\omega}_2$ are required only to lie in the plane perpendicular to $\boldsymbol{\omega}_3$. Consequently, there is no loss of generality if we also choose $\boldsymbol{\omega}_1 \cdot \boldsymbol{\omega}_2 = 0$. Thus, the principal axes for a rigid body with an axis of symmetry can also be chosen to be an orthogonal set.

We have previously shown that the principal moments of inertia are obtained as the roots of the secular equation—a cubic equation. Mathematically, at least one of the roots of a cubic equation must be real, but there may be two imaginary roots. If the diagonalization procedures for the inertia tensor are to be physically meaningful, we must always obtain only real values for the principal moments. We can show in the following way that this is a general result. First, we assume the roots to be complex and use a procedure similar to that used in the preceding proof. But now we must also allow the quantities $\omega_{im}$ to become complex. We therefore write Equation 11.81a as before, but we take the complex conjugate of Equation 11.81b:

$$\sum_k I_{ik} \omega_{km} = I_m \omega_{im}$$
$$\sum_i I_{ki} \omega_{in}^* = I_n^* \omega_{kn}^*$$

Next, we multiply the first of these equations by $\omega_{in}^*$ and sum over $i$, and multiply the second by $\omega_{km}$ and sum over $k$. The inertia tensor is symmetrical, and its elements are all real, so that $I_{ik} = I_{ki}$. Therefore, subtracting the second of these equations from the first, we find:

$$(I_m - I_n^*) \sum_j \omega_{jm} \omega_{jn}^* = 0$$
*(11.88)*

For the case $m = n$, we have:

$$(I_n - I_n^*) \sum_j \omega_{jn} \omega_{jn}^* = 0$$
*(11.89)*

The sum is just the definition of the squared magnitude of $\boldsymbol{\omega}_n$:

$$\sum_j \omega_{jn} \omega_{jn}^* = |\boldsymbol{\omega}_n|^2 > 0$$
*(11.90)*

Therefore, because the squared magnitude of $\boldsymbol{\omega}_n$ is positive, it must be true that:

$$I_n - I_n^* = 0$$

for Equation 11.89 to be satisfied. If a quantity and its complex conjugate are equal, then the imaginary parts must vanish identically. Thus, the principal moments of inertia are all real. Because $\{\mathbf{I}\}$ is real, the vectors $\boldsymbol{\omega}_n$ must also be real.

If $m \neq n$ in Equation 11.88 and if $I_m \neq I_n^*$, then the equation can be satisfied only if $\sum_j \omega_{jm} \omega_{jn}^* = 0$; that is, these vectors are orthogonal.

In all the proofs carried out in this section, we have referred to the inertia tensor. But examining these proofs reveals that the only properties of the inertia tensor that have actually been used are the facts that the tensor is symmetrical and that the elements are real. We may therefore conclude that any real, symmetric tensor[^9] has the following properties:
1. Diagonalization may be accomplished by an appropriate rotation of axes (a similarity transformation).
2. The eigenvalues are obtained as roots of the secular determinant and are real.
3. The eigenvectors are real and orthogonal.

[^8]: We confine our attention to rectangular coordinate systems so that we may ignore some of the more complicated properties of tensors that manifest themselves in general curvilinear coordinates.
[^9]: In mathematical terminology, a real symmetric matrix or a self-adjoint operator.
