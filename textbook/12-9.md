## 12.9 The Loaded String

### Original Transcribed Notes

We now consider a more complex system consisting of an elastic string (or a spring) on which a number of identical particles are placed at regular intervals. The ends of the string are constrained to remain stationary. Let the mass of each of the $n$ particles be $m$, and let the spacing between particles at equilibrium be $d$. The total length of the string is therefore $L = (n + 1)d$.

**Figure 12-10 Context:** Vertical displacements ($q_{j-1}$, $q_j$, and $q_{j+1}$) of masses on the loaded string.

If we consider only the transverse displacements $q_j$ of the particles, we can write the equation of motion for the $j$th particle using Newton's second law. The tension $\tau$ in the string is assumed to be constant for small displacements. The forces acting on the $j$th particle are due to the tension in the two adjacent segments of the string. The angles that these segments make with the equilibrium line are approximately $(q_j - q_{j-1})/d$ and $(q_j - q_{j+1})/d$. Therefore, the net restoring force in the transverse direction is

$$F_j = -\frac{\tau}{d}(q_j - q_{j-1}) - \frac{\tau}{d}(q_j - q_{j+1}) = \frac{\tau}{d}(q_{j-1} - 2q_j + q_{j+1})$$
*(12.130)*

which yields the equation of motion:

$$m\ddot{q}_j - \frac{\tau}{d}(q_{j-1} - 2q_j + q_{j+1}) = 0$$
*(12.131)*

The system is coupled because the force on the $j$th particle depends on the positions of the $(j-1)$th and $(j+1)$th particles; this is therefore an example of nearest neighbor interaction, in which the coupling is only to the adjacent particles. It is not necessary that the interaction be confined to nearest neighbors. If the force between pairs of particles were electrostatic, for example, then each particle would be coupled to all the other particles. The problem can then become quite complicated. But even if the force is electrostatic, the $1/r^2$ dependence on distance frequently permits us to neglect interactions at distances greater than one interparticle spacing, so that the simple expression for the force given in Equation 12.130 is approximately correct.

We have considered only the motion perpendicular to the line of the string: transverse oscillations. It is easy to show that exactly the same form for the equations of motion results if we consider longitudinal vibrations—that is, motions along the line of the string. In this case, the factor $\tau/d$ is replaced by the force constant $\kappa$ of the string (see Problem 12-24).

Although we used Newton's equations to obtain the equations of motion (Equation 12.131), we may equally well use the Lagrangian method. The potential energy arises from the work done to stretch the $n + 1$ string segments:[^15]

$$U = \frac{\tau}{2d} \sum_{j=1}^{n+1} (q_j - q_{j-1})^2$$
*(12.132)*

where $q_0$ and $q_{n+1}$ are identically zero, because these positions correspond to the fixed ends of the string. We note that Equation 12.132 yields an expression for the force on the $j$th particle that is the same as the previous result (Equation 12.130):

$$F_j = -\frac{\partial U}{\partial q_j} = \frac{\tau}{d}(q_{j-1} - 2q_j + q_{j+1})$$
*(12.133)*

The kinetic energy is given by the sum of the kinetic energies of the $n$ individual particles:

$$T = \frac{1}{2} \sum_{j=1}^{n} m\dot{q}_j^2$$
*(12.134)*

Because $\dot{q}_{n+1} = 0$, we may extend the sum in Equation 12.134 to $j = n + 1$ so that the range of $j$ is the same as that in the expression for the potential energy. Then, the Lagrangian becomes

$$L = \frac{1}{2} \sum_{j=1}^{n+1} \left[ m\dot{q}_j^2 - \frac{\tau}{d}(q_j - q_{j-1})^2 \right]$$
*(12.135)*

It should be obvious that the equation of motion for the $j$th particle must arise from only those terms in the Lagrangian containing $q_j$ or $\dot{q}_j$. If we expand the sum in $L$, we find

$$L = \frac{1}{2} m\dot{q}_j^2 - \frac{\tau}{2d} \left[ (q_j - q_{j-1})^2 + (q_{j+1} - q_j)^2 \right] + \dots$$
*(12.136)*

where we have written only those terms that contain either $q_j$ or $\dot{q}_j$. Applying Lagrange's equation for the coordinate $q_j$, we have

$$m\ddot{q}_j - \frac{\tau}{d}(q_{j-1} - 2q_j + q_{j+1}) = 0$$
*(12.137)*

Thus, the result is the same as that obtained by using the Newtonian method.

To solve the equations of motion, we substitute, as usual,

$$q_j(t) = a_j e^{i\omega t}$$
*(12.138)*

where $a_j$ can be complex. Substituting this expression for $q_j(t)$ into Equation 12.137, we find

$$\frac{\tau}{d}a_{j-1} + \left( m\omega^2 - \frac{2\tau}{d} \right)a_j + \frac{\tau}{d}a_{j+1} = 0$$
*(12.139)*

where $j = 1, 2, \dots, n$, but because the ends of the string are fixed, we must have $a_0 = a_{n+1} = 0$.

Equation 12.139 represents a linear difference equation that can be solved for the eigenfrequencies $\omega_r$ by setting the determinant of the coefficients equal to zero. We therefore have the following secular determinant:

$$\begin{vmatrix}
\frac{2\tau}{d} - m\omega^2 & -\frac{\tau}{d} & 0 & 0 & \dots \\
-\frac{\tau}{d} & \frac{2\tau}{d} - m\omega^2 & -\frac{\tau}{d} & 0 & \dots \\
0 & -\frac{\tau}{d} & \frac{2\tau}{d} - m\omega^2 & -\frac{\tau}{d} & \dots \\
\vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \ddots
\end{vmatrix} = 0$$
*(12.140)*

where we have used

$$A_{jj} = \frac{2\tau}{d}, \quad A_{j,j-1} = A_{j,j+1} = -\frac{\tau}{d}, \quad m_{jj} = m, \quad m_{j,j-1} = 0$$
*(12.141)*

This secular determinant is a special case of the general determinant (Equation 12.42) that results if the tensor $\{\mathbf{m}\}$ is diagonal and the tensor $\{\mathbf{A}\}$ involves a coupling only between adjacent particles. Thus, Equation 12.140 consists only of diagonal elements plus elements once-removed from the diagonal.

For the case $n = 1$ (i.e., a single mass suspended between two identical springs), we have $a_0 = a_2 = 0$, or

$$\omega = \sqrt{\frac{2\tau}{md}}$$

We may adapt this result to the case of longitudinal motion by replacing $\tau/d$ by $\kappa$; we then obtain the familiar expression,

$$\omega = \sqrt{\frac{2\kappa}{m}}$$

For the case $n = 2$, and with $\tau/d$ replaced by $\kappa$, we have

$$\omega_1 = \sqrt{\frac{\kappa}{m}}, \quad \omega_2 = \sqrt{\frac{3\kappa}{m}}$$

which are the same frequencies as those found in Section 12.2 for two coupled masses (Equations 12.7 and 12.8) when the coupling spring is identical to the outer springs ($\kappa_{12} = \kappa$).

The secular equation should be relatively easy to solve directly for small values of $n$, but the solution becomes quite complicated for large $n$. In such cases, it is simpler to use the following method. We try a solution of the form

$$a_j = a e^{i(j\gamma - \delta)}$$
*(12.142)*

where $a$, $\gamma$, and $\delta$ are real. The use of this trial solution is justified if we can find a quantity $\gamma$ and a phase $\delta$ such that the conditions of the problem are all satisfied. Substituting $a_j$ in this form into Equation 12.139 and canceling the common exponential and phase factor, we find

$$\frac{\tau}{d} e^{-i\gamma} + \left( m\omega^2 - \frac{2\tau}{d} \right) + \frac{\tau}{d} e^{i\gamma} = 0$$

Solving for $\omega^2$, we obtain

$$\omega^2 = \frac{2\tau}{md} (1 - \cos \gamma) = \frac{4\tau}{md} \sin^2 \frac{\gamma}{2}$$
*(12.143)*

Because we know that the secular determinant is of order $n$ and therefore yields exactly $n$ values for $\omega_r^2$, we can write

$$\omega_r = 2 \sqrt{\frac{\tau}{md}} \sin \frac{\gamma_r}{2}, \quad r = 1, 2, \dots, n$$
*(12.144)*

We now evaluate the quantity $\gamma_r$ and the phase $\delta_r$ by applying the boundary conditions that the ends of the string remain fixed. Thus, we have

$$a_0 = a_{n+1} = 0$$
*(12.145)*

or, because only the real part is physically meaningful,

$$a_j = a \cos(j\gamma - \delta)$$
*(12.146)*

The boundary condition $a_0 = 0$ requires

$$a \cos(-\delta) = 0 \implies \delta = \frac{\pi}{2}$$
*(12.147)*

Hence,

$$a_j = a \cos\left( j\gamma - \frac{\pi}{2} \right) = a \sin j\gamma$$
*(12.148)*

For $j = n + 1$, we have

$$a_{n+1} = 0 = a \sin(n+1)\gamma$$

Therefore,

$$(n + 1)\gamma_s = s\pi, \quad s = 1, 2, \dots$$

or

$$\gamma_s = \frac{s\pi}{n + 1}$$

But there are just $n$ distinct values of $\gamma_r$ because Equation 12.144 requires $n$ distinct values of $\omega_r$. Therefore, the index $s$ runs from $1$ to $n$. Because there is a one-to-one correspondence between the values of $s$ and the values of $r$, we can simply replace $s$ in this last expression by the index $r$:

$$\gamma_r = \frac{r\pi}{n + 1}, \quad r = 1, 2, \dots, n$$
*(12.149)*

The $a_{jr}$ (Equation 12.148) then becomes

$$a_{jr} = a_r \sin\left( \frac{jr\pi}{n + 1} \right)$$
*(12.150)*

The general solution for $q_j(t)$ (see Equation 12.61) is

$$q_j(t) = \sum_r \beta_r a_{jr} e^{i\omega_r t} = \sum_r \beta_r \sin\left( \frac{jr\pi}{n + 1} \right) e^{i\omega_r t}$$
*(12.151)*

where we have written $\beta_r = B_r a_r$. Furthermore, for the frequency $\omega_r$ we have

$$\omega_r = 2\sqrt{\frac{\tau}{md}} \sin\left[ \frac{r\pi}{2(n + 1)} \right]$$
*(12.152)*

We note that this expression yields the same results found for the case of two coupled oscillators (Equations 12.7 and 12.8) when we insert $n = 2, r = 1, 2$ and replace $\tau/d$ by $\kappa$.

Notice also that if either $r = 0$ or $r = n + 1$ is substituted into Equation 12.150, then all the amplitude factors $a_{jr}$ vanish identically. These values of $r$ therefore refer to null modes. Moreover, if $r$ takes on the values $n + 2, n + 3, \dots, 2n + 1$, then the $a_{jr}$ are the same (except for a trivial sign change and in reverse order) as for $r = 1, 2, \dots, n$; also, $r = 2n + 2$ yields the next null mode. We conclude, therefore, that there are indeed only $n$ distinct modes and that increasing $r$ beyond $n$ merely duplicates the modes for smaller $r$. (A similar argument applies for $r < 0$.)

These conclusions are illustrated in Figure 12-11 for the case $n = 3$. The distinct modes are specified by $r = 1, 2, 3$; $r = 4$ is a null mode. The displacement patterns are duplicated for $r = 7, 6, 5, 8$, but with a change in sign. In Figure 12-11, the dashed curves merely represent the sinusoidal behavior of the amplitude factors $a_{jr}$ for various values of $r$; the only physically meaningful features of these curves are the values at the positions occupied by the particles ($j = 1, 2, 3$). The "high frequency" of the sine curves for $r = 5, 6, 7, 8$ is thus not at all related to the frequency of the particles' motions; these latter frequencies are the same as for $r = 1, 2, 3, 4$.

The normal coordinates of the system (Equation 12.62) are

$$\eta_r(t) = \beta_r e^{i\omega_r t}$$
*(12.153)*

so that

$$q_j(t) = \sum_r \eta_r(t) \sin\left( \frac{jr\pi}{n + 1} \right)$$
*(12.154)*

This equation for $q_j(t)$ is similar to the previous expression (Equation 12.63) except that the quantities $a_{jr}$ are now replaced by $\sin[jr\pi/(n + 1)]$.

Because $\beta_r$ may be complex, we write for the real part of $q_j(t)$

$$q_j(t) = \sum_{r=1}^{n} \sin\left( \frac{jr\pi}{n + 1} \right) \left[ \mu_r \cos \omega_r t + \nu_r \sin \omega_r t \right]$$
*(12.155)*

where

$$\mu_r = \rho_r \cos \delta_r, \quad \nu_r = -\rho_r \sin \delta_r$$
*(12.156)*

The initial value of $q_j(t)$ and $\dot{q}_j(t)$ can be obtained from Equation 12.155:

$$q_j(0) = \sum_{r=1}^{n} \mu_r \sin\left( \frac{jr\pi}{n + 1} \right)$$
*(12.157)*

$$\dot{q}_j(0) = \sum_{r=1}^{n} \omega_r \nu_r \sin\left( \frac{jr\pi}{n + 1} \right)$$
*(12.158)*

If we multiply Equation 12.157 by $\sin[j s\pi/(n + 1)]$ and sum over $j$, we find

$$\sum_{j=1}^{n} q_j(0) \sin\left( \frac{j s\pi}{n + 1} \right) = \sum_{r=1}^{n} \mu_r \sum_{j=1}^{n} \sin\left( \frac{jr\pi}{n + 1} \right) \sin\left( \frac{j s\pi}{n + 1} \right)$$
*(12.159)*

A relationship in the form of a trigonometric identity is available for the sine terms:

$$\sum_{j=1}^{n} \sin\left( \frac{jr\pi}{n + 1} \right) \sin\left( \frac{j s\pi}{n + 1} \right) = \frac{n + 1}{2} \delta_{rs}$$
*(12.160)*

so that Equation 12.159 becomes

$$\sum_{j=1}^{n} q_j(0) \sin\left( \frac{jr\pi}{n + 1} \right) = \mu_r \left( \frac{n + 1}{2} \right)$$

or

$$\mu_r = \frac{2}{n + 1} \sum_{j=1}^{n} q_j(0) \sin\left( \frac{jr\pi}{n + 1} \right)$$
*(12.161a)*

A similar procedure for $\nu_r$ yields

$$\nu_r = \frac{2}{(n + 1)\omega_r} \sum_{j=1}^{n} \dot{q}_j(0) \sin\left( \frac{jr\pi}{n + 1} \right)$$
*(12.161b)*

Thus, we have evaluated all the necessary quantities, and the description of the vibrations of a loaded string is therefore complete.

We should note the following point regarding the normalization procedures used here. First, in Equation 12.57 we arbitrarily normalized the $a_{jr}$ to unity. Thus, the $a_{jr}$ are required to be independent of the initial conditions imposed on the system. The scale factors $\alpha_r$ and $\beta_r$ then allowed the magnitude of the oscillations to be varied by the selection of the initial conditions. Next, in the problem of the loaded string, we found that instead of the quantities $a_{jr}$, there arose the sine functions $\sin[jr\pi/(n + 1)]$, and these functions possess a normalization property (Equation 12.160) that is specified by trigonometric identities. Therefore, in this case it is not possible arbitrarily to impose a normalization condition; we are automatically presented with the condition. But this is no restriction; it means only that the scale factors $\beta_r$ for this case have a slightly different form. Thus, there are certain constants that occur in the two problems that, for convenience, are separated in different ways in the two cases.

**EXAMPLE 12.7** Consider a loaded string consisting of three particles regularly spaced on the string. At $t = 0$ the center particle (only) is displaced a distance $a$ and released from rest. Describe the subsequent motion.

**Solution.** The initial conditions are

$$q_2(0) = a, \quad q_1(0) = q_3(0) = 0$$
$$\dot{q}_1(0) = \dot{q}_2(0) = \dot{q}_3(0) = 0$$

Because the initial velocities are zero, the $\nu_r$ vanish. The $\mu_r$ are given by (Equation 12.161a):

$$\mu_r = \frac{2}{3 + 1} \sum_{j=1}^{3} q_j(0) \sin\left( \frac{jr\pi}{4} \right) = \frac{1}{2} a \sin\left( \frac{2r\pi}{4} \right) = \frac{1}{2} a \sin\left( \frac{r\pi}{2} \right)$$
*(12.162)*

because only the term $j = 2$ contributes to the sum. Thus,

$$\mu_1 = \frac{1}{2} a, \quad \mu_2 = 0, \quad \mu_3 = -\frac{1}{2} a$$
*(12.163)*

The quantities $\sin[jr\pi/(n + 1)]$ that appear in the expression for $q_j(t)$ (Equation 12.155) are

$$\sin\left( \frac{jr\pi}{4} \right)$$
*(12.164)*

The displacements of the three particles therefore are:

$$q_1(t) = \frac{a}{2\sqrt{2}} (\cos \omega_1 t - \cos \omega_3 t)$$
$$q_2(t) = \frac{a}{2} (\cos \omega_1 t + \cos \omega_3 t)$$
$$q_3(t) = \frac{a}{2\sqrt{2}} (\cos \omega_1 t - \cos \omega_3 t) = q_1(t)$$
*(12.165)*

where the characteristic frequencies are given by Equation 12.152:

$$\omega_r = 2\sqrt{\frac{\tau}{md}} \sin\left( \frac{r\pi}{8} \right), \quad r = 1, 2, 3$$
*(12.166)*

Notice that because the middle particle was initially displaced, no vibration mode occurs in which this particle is at rest; that is, mode 2 with frequency $\omega_2$ (see Figure 12-11) is absent.

[^15]: We consider the potential energy to be only the elastic energy in the string; that is, we do not consider the individual masses to have any gravitational (or any other) potential energy.
