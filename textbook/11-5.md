## 11.5 Principal Axes of Inertia

### Original Transcribed Notes

It should be clear that a considerable simplification in the expressions for $T$ and $\mathbf{L}$ would result if the inertia tensor consisted only of diagonal elements. If we could write:

$$I_{ij} = I_i \delta_{ij}$$
*(11.32)*

then the inertia tensor would be diagonal:

$$\{\mathbf{I}\} = \begin{pmatrix} I_1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & I_2 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & I_3 \end{pmatrix}$$
*(11.33)*

We would then have:

$$L_i = I_i \omega_i$$
*(11.34)*

and:

$$T_{\text{rot}} = \frac{1}{2} \sum_i I_i \omega_i^2$$
*(11.35)*

Thus, the condition that $\{\mathbf{I}\}$ has only diagonal elements provides quite simple expressions for the angular momentum and the rotational kinetic energy. We now determine the conditions under which Equation 11.32 becomes the description of the inertia tensor. This involves finding a set of body axes for which the products of inertia (i.e., the off-diagonal elements of $\{\mathbf{I}\}$) vanish. We call such axes the principal axes of inertia.[^4]

If a body rotates around a principal axis, both the angular velocity and the angular momentum are, according to Equation 11.34, directed along this axis. Then, if $I$ is the rotational inertia (moment of inertia) about this axis, we can write:

$$\mathbf{L} = I \boldsymbol{\omega}$$
*(11.36)*

Equating the components of $\mathbf{L}$ in Equations 11.20a and 11.36, we have:

$$L_1 = I\omega_1 = I_{11}\omega_1 + I_{12}\omega_2 + I_{13}\omega_3$$
$$L_2 = I\omega_2 = I_{21}\omega_1 + I_{22}\omega_2 + I_{23}\omega_3$$
$$L_3 = I\omega_3 = I_{31}\omega_1 + I_{32}\omega_2 + I_{33}\omega_3$$
*(11.37)*

Or, collecting terms, we obtain:

$$(I_{11} - I)\omega_1 + I_{12}\omega_2 + I_{13}\omega_3 = 0$$
$$I_{21}\omega_1 + (I_{22} - I)\omega_2 + I_{23}\omega_3 = 0$$
$$I_{31}\omega_1 + I_{32}\omega_2 + (I_{33} - I)\omega_3 = 0$$
*(11.38)*

The condition that these equations have a nontrivial solution is that the determinant of the coefficients vanishes:

$$\begin{vmatrix} I_{11} - I & I_{12} & I_{13} \\ I_{21} & I_{22} - I & I_{23} \\ I_{31} & I_{32} & I_{33} - I \end{vmatrix} = 0$$
*(11.39)*

The expansion of this determinant leads to the secular or characteristic equation[^5] for $I$, which is a cubic equation. Each of the three roots corresponds to a moment of inertia about one of the principal axes. These values, $I_1$, $I_2$, and $I_3$, are called the principal moments of inertia. If the body rotates about the axis corresponding to the principal moment $I_r$, then Equation 11.36 becomes $\mathbf{L} = I_r \boldsymbol{\omega}$—that is, both $\boldsymbol{\omega}$ and $\mathbf{L}$ are directed along this axis. The direction of $\boldsymbol{\omega}$ with respect to the body coordinate system is then the same as the direction of the principal axis corresponding to $I_r$. Therefore, we can determine the direction of this principal axis by substituting $I_r$ for $I$ in Equation 11.38 and determining the ratios of the components of the angular velocity vector: $\omega_1 : \omega_2 : \omega_3$. We thereby determine the direction cosines of the axis about which the moment of inertia is $I_r$. The directions corresponding to the other roots can be found in a similar fashion. That the principal axes determined in this manner are indeed real and orthogonal is proved in Section 11.7; these results also follow from the more general considerations given in Section 12.4.

The fact that the diagonalization procedure just described yields only the ratios of the components of $\boldsymbol{\omega}$ is no handicap, because the ratios completely determine the direction of each of the principal axes, and it is only the directions of these axes that are required. Indeed, we would not expect the magnitudes of the $\omega_i$ to be determined, because the actual rate of the body's angular motion cannot be specified by the geometry alone. We are free to impress on the body any magnitude of the angular velocity we wish.

For most of the problems encountered in rigid-body dynamics, the bodies are of some regular shape, so we can determine the principal axes merely by examining the symmetry of the body. For example, any body that is a solid of revolution (e.g., a cylindrical rod) has one principal axis that lies along the symmetry axis, and the other two axes are in a plane perpendicular to the symmetry axis. It should be obvious that because the body is symmetrical, the choice of the angular placement of these other two axes is arbitrary. If the moment of inertia along the symmetry axis is $I_3$, then $I_1 = I_2$ for a solid of revolution—that is, the secular equation has a double root.

If a body has $I_1 = I_2 = I_3$, it is termed a spherical top; if $I_1 = I_2 \neq I_3$, it is termed a symmetric top; if the principal moments of inertia are all distinct, it is termed an asymmetric top. If a body has $I_1 = I_2$ and $I_3 = 0$, as, for example, two point masses connected by a weightless shaft, or a diatomic molecule, it is called a rotor.

**EXAMPLE 11.5** Find the principal moments of inertia and the principal axes for the cube in Example 11.3.

**Solution.** In Example 11.3, we found that the moment-of-inertia tensor for a cube (with origin at one corner) had nonzero off-diagonal elements. Evidently, the coordinate axes chosen for that calculation were not principal axes. If, for example, the cube rotates about the $x_3$-axis, then $\boldsymbol{\omega} = \omega_3 \mathbf{e}_3$ and the angular momentum vector $\mathbf{L}$ (see Equation 11.37) has the components:

$$L_1 = -\frac{1}{4} \beta \omega_3$$
$$L_2 = -\frac{1}{4} \beta \omega_3$$
$$L_3 = \frac{2}{3} \beta \omega_3$$

where $\beta = M b^2$. Thus, $\mathbf{L}$ is not in the same direction as $\boldsymbol{\omega}$.

To find the principal moments of inertia, we must solve the secular equation:

$$\begin{vmatrix} \frac{2}{3}\beta - I & -\frac{1}{4}\beta & -\frac{1}{4}\beta \\ -\frac{1}{4}\beta & \frac{2}{3}\beta - I & -\frac{1}{4}\beta \\ -\frac{1}{4}\beta & -\frac{1}{4}\beta & \frac{2}{3}\beta - I \end{vmatrix} = 0$$
*(11.40)*

The value of a determinant is not affected by adding (or subtracting) any row (or column) from any other row (or column). Equation 11.40 can be solved more easily if we subtract the first row from the second:

$$\begin{vmatrix} \frac{2}{3}\beta - I & -\frac{1}{4}\beta & -\frac{1}{4}\beta \\ -\left( \frac{11}{12}\beta - I \right) & \frac{11}{12}\beta - I & 0 \\ -\frac{1}{4}\beta & -\frac{1}{4}\beta & \frac{2}{3}\beta - I \end{vmatrix} = 0$$

We can factor $\left( \frac{11}{12}\beta - I \right)$ from the second row, which leads to the factored characteristic equation:

$$\left( \frac{11}{12}\beta - I \right)^2 \left( \frac{1}{6}\beta - I \right) = 0$$

Thus, we have the following roots, which give the principal moments of inertia:

$$I_1 = I_2 = \frac{11}{12}\beta = \frac{11}{12}Mb^2$$
$$I_3 = \frac{1}{6}\beta = \frac{1}{6}Mb^2$$

The diagonalized moment-of-inertia tensor becomes:

$$\{\mathbf{I}\} = Mb^2 \begin{pmatrix} 11/12 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 11/12 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1/6 \end{pmatrix}$$
*(11.41)*

Because two of the roots are identical, $I_1 = I_2$, the principal axis associated with $I_3$ must be an axis of symmetry.

To find the direction of the principal axis associated with $I_3$, we substitute the value $I = I_3 = \frac{1}{6}\beta$ into Equation 11.38:

$$\left(\frac{2}{3}\beta - \frac{1}{6}\beta\right)\omega_{13} - \frac{1}{4}\beta\omega_{23} - \frac{1}{4}\beta\omega_{33} = 0$$
$$-\frac{1}{4}\beta\omega_{13} + \left(\frac{2}{3}\beta - \frac{1}{6}\beta\right)\omega_{23} - \frac{1}{4}\beta\omega_{33} = 0$$
$$-\frac{1}{4}\beta\omega_{13} - \frac{1}{4}\beta\omega_{23} + \left(\frac{2}{3}\beta - \frac{1}{6}\beta\right)\omega_{33} = 0$$

where the second subscript $3$ on the $\omega_i$ signifies that we are considering the principal axis associated with $I_3$. Dividing these equations by $\beta/4$, we have:

$$2\omega_{13} - \omega_{23} - \omega_{33} = 0$$
$$-\omega_{13} + 2\omega_{23} - \omega_{33} = 0$$
$$-\omega_{13} - \omega_{23} + 2\omega_{33} = 0$$
*(11.42)*

Subtracting the second of these equations from the first, we find $\omega_{13} = \omega_{23}$. Using this result in either of the Equations 11.42, we obtain $\omega_{13} = \omega_{23} = \omega_{33}$, and the desired ratios are:

$$\omega_{13} : \omega_{23} : \omega_{33} = 1 : 1 : 1$$

Therefore, when the cube rotates about an axis that has associated with it the moment of inertia $I_3 = \frac{1}{6}Mb^2$, the projections of $\boldsymbol{\omega}$ on the three coordinate axes are all equal. Hence, this principal axis corresponds to the diagonal of the cube.

Because the moments $I_1$ and $I_2$ are equal, the orientation of the principal axes associated with these moments is arbitrary; they need only lie in a plane normal to the diagonal of the cube.

[^4]: Discovered by Euler in 1750.
[^5]: So called because a similar equation describes secular perturbations in celestial mechanics. The mathematical terminology is the characteristic polynomial.
