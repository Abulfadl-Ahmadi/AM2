

### **Elaboration: Expanding on the Concepts**

> **Why the Center of Mass Matters:**
> 
> The Center of Mass (CM) is one of the most powerful concepts in classical mechanics. It represents the unique point in space where the entire mass of a system can be considered to be concentrated for the purpose of analyzing its translational motion. Even if a system consists of billions of moving particles (like a gas) or is an extended rigid body (like a thrown wrench), the CM of that system will move exactly as a single point particle would if subjected to the same net external force.
> 
> **Discrete vs. Continuous Systems:**
> 
> - **Discrete Systems (Equation 9.3):** This formula is a weighted average. You multiply the position vector $r_{\alpha}$ of each individual particle by its mass $m_{\alpha}$, add them all up, and divide by the total mass $M$.
>     
> - **Continuous Systems (Equation 9.4):** Real objects are made of a near-infinite number of atoms, so summing them individually is impossible. We use calculus to take the limit as the mass of each "particle" becomes infinitesimally small ($dm$). The summation $\sum$ naturally transitions into an integral $\int$.
>     
> 
> **The Power of Symmetry (Example 9.1):**
> 
> Example 9.1 provides a classic demonstration of working smart, not hard. By placing the center of the hemisphere's base precisely at the origin $(0,0,0)$ of the coordinate system, the $x$ and $z$ axes slice the hemisphere into perfectly symmetrical halves.
> 
> Mathematically, calculating $X$ requires integrating the function $f(x) = x$ across the interval $[-a, a]$. Because $f(x) = x$ is an _odd function_ (meaning $f(-x) = -f(x)$), the integral over a symmetric interval is exactly zero. The positive mass contributions on one side of the axis perfectly cancel the negative mass contributions on the other. This lets you immediately declare $X = 0$ and $Z = 0$ without doing any calculus.
> 
> **Breaking Down the Calculus for the $Y$ Coordinate:**
> 
> Let's look closely at how the text sets up the integral for $Y$. Since the hemisphere lies only in the positive $y$-direction (from $y=0$ to $y=a$), there is no symmetry to cancel things out. We must integrate.
> 
> 1. **Defining the differential mass ($dm$):** Instead of using a tiny 3D box ($dx~dy~dz$) for the volume, the text uses a flat, circular slice (a disk) parallel to the $xz$-plane at a height $y$.
>     
> 2. **Finding the Volume of the Disk ($dV$):** The thickness of this disk is $dy$. The radius of this disk depends on how high up it is. By the Pythagorean theorem, the radius $r$ at height $y$ is related to the hemisphere's total radius $a$ by $r^2 + y^2 = a^2$. Therefore, $r^2 = a^2 - y^2$. The volume of the thin disk is Area $\times$ thickness:
>     
>     $$dV = (\pi r^2) dy = \pi (a^2 - y^2) dy$$
>     
> 3. **Integrating:** Substituting $dm = \rho dV$ into the $Y$ center of mass formula:
>     
>     $$Y = \frac{1}{M} \int_{0}^{a} y [\rho \pi (a^2 - y^2) dy]$$
>     
>     Pulling out the constants ($\rho$ and $\pi$) and distributing the $y$:
>     
>     $$Y = \frac{\rho \pi}{M} \int_{0}^{a} (a^2 y - y^3) dy$$
>     
>     $$Y = \frac{\rho \pi}{M} \left[ \frac{a^2 y^2}{2} - \frac{y^4}{4} \right]_{0}^{a}$$
>     
>     $$Y = \frac{\rho \pi}{M} \left( \frac{a^4}{2} - \frac{a^4}{4} \right) = \frac{\rho \pi a^4}{4 M}$$
>     
> 1. **Final Substitution:** Recall that the mass $M$ of a hemisphere is volume $\times$ density: $M = \frac{2}{3} \pi a^3 \rho$. Substituting this into the denominator gives the final result: $\frac{3a}{8}$. This tells us the center of mass is a little less than halfway up the hemisphere, which makes physical sense as there is more mass concentrated near the wide base.


## 9.3 Linear Momentum of the System

> **Original Notes & Transcription**
> 
> If a certain group of particles constitutes a system, then the resultant force acting on a particle within the system (say, the $\alpha$th particle) is in general composed of two parts. One part is the resultant of all forces whose origin lies outside of the system; this is called the external force, $F_{\alpha}^{(e)}$. The other part is the resultant of the forces arising from the interaction of all of the other $n-1$ particles with the $\alpha$th particle; this is called the internal force, $f_{\alpha}$. Force $f_{\alpha}$ is given by the vector sum of all the individual forces $f_{\alpha\beta}$,
> 
> $$f_{\alpha}=\sum_{\beta}f_{\alpha\beta}$$
> 
> (9.5) where $f_{\alpha\beta}$ represents the force on the $\alpha$th particle due to the $\beta$th particle. The total force acting on the $\alpha$th particle is therefore
> 
> $$F_{\alpha}=F_{\alpha}^{(e)}+f_{\alpha}$$
> 
> (9.6) Also, according to the weak statement of Newton's Third Law, we have
> 
> $$f_{\alpha\beta}=-f_{\beta\alpha}$$
> 
> (9.1)

**Elaboration:**

When analyzing a system containing multiple particles (like a gas, a rigid body, or a galaxy), it becomes essential to categorize forces to simplify the math.

- **External Forces ($F_{\alpha}^{(e)}$):** These are forces originating from outside the defined system boundaries (e.g., gravity from the Earth pulling on a falling object, or an applied electric field).
    
- **Internal Forces ($f_{\alpha}$):** These are the action-reaction pairs between the particles _within_ the system itself (e.g., the molecular bonds holding a solid together, or the gravitational attraction between two stars in a binary system).
    
    By explicitly invoking the **weak form of Newton's Third Law** ($f_{\alpha\beta}=-f_{\beta\alpha}$), we are setting up the mathematical framework to prove a profound physical concept: internal forces perfectly cancel each other out and therefore have absolutely no effect on the macroscopic motion of the system as a whole.
    

### Applying Newton's Second Law to the System

> **Original Notes & Transcription**
> 
> Newton's Second Law for the $\alpha$th particle can be written as
> 
> $$\dot{p}_{\alpha}=m_{\alpha}\ddot{r}_{\alpha}=F_{\alpha}^{(e)}+f_{\alpha}$$
> 
> (9.7) or
> 
> $$\frac{d^{2}}{dt^{2}}(m_{\alpha}r_{\alpha})=F_{\alpha}^{(e)}+\sum_{\beta}f_{\alpha\beta}$$
> 
> (9.8) Summing this expression over $\alpha$, we have
> 
> $$\frac{d^{2}}{dt^{2}}\sum_{\alpha}m_{\alpha}r_{\alpha}=\sum_{\alpha}F_{\alpha}^{(e)}+\sum_{\alpha}\sum_{\alpha\neq\beta}f_{\alpha\beta}$$
> 
> (9.9) where the terms $\alpha=\beta$ do not enter in the second sum on the right-hand side, because $f_{\alpha\alpha}\equiv0$. The summation on the left-hand side just yields $MR$ (see Equation 9.3), and the second time derivative is $M\ddot{R}$. The first term on the right-hand side is the sum of all the external forces and can be written as
> 
> $$\sum_{\alpha}F_{\alpha}^{(e)}\equiv F$$
> 
> (9.10) The second term on the right-hand side in Equation 9.9 can be expressed as
> 
> $$\sum_{\alpha}\sum_{\alpha\neq\beta}f_{\alpha\beta}\equiv\sum_{\alpha,\beta\neq\alpha}f_{\alpha\beta}=\sum_{\alpha<\beta}(f_{\alpha\beta}+f_{\beta\alpha})$$
> 
> which vanishes according to Equation 9.1. Thus, we have the first important result
> 
> $$M\ddot{R}=F$$
> 
> (9.11)

**Elaboration:**

This derivation is the cornerstone of rigid body dynamics. Here is a breakdown of the professor's likely verbal explanation for the mathematical steps:

1. **Summing over all particles:** Equation 9.8 is just $F = ma$ for a single particle. To find the behavior of the whole system, we sum this equation over every single particle $\alpha$ from $1$ to $n$ (Equation 9.9).
    
2. **The Left-Hand Side:** The term $\sum_{\alpha} m_{\alpha}r_{\alpha}$ is exactly the definition of the Center of Mass position ($R$) multiplied by the total mass ($M$). Taking two time derivatives gives $M\ddot{R}$, which is the mass times the acceleration of the Center of Mass.
    
3. **The Double Summation Trick:** The term $\sum_{\alpha}\sum_{\alpha\neq\beta}f_{\alpha\beta}$ represents the sum of all internal forces. The text notes that these summation indices ($\alpha$ and $\beta$) are "dummy indices," meaning their specific labels don't matter. If you write out the terms, for every force $f_{12}$ (force on 1 by 2), there is a corresponding force $f_{21}$ (force on 2 by 1). Because of Newton's Third Law ($f_{12} = -f_{21}$), every single pair in this massive sum perfectly cancels out to zero.
    
4. **The Result:** Equation 9.11 ($M\ddot{R}=F$) mathematically proves that you can treat a complex system (like a spinning, wobbling wrench thrown through the air) as if all its mass were concentrated at a single point (the Center of Mass), and only the net external force dictates the acceleration of that specific point.
    

> **Original Notes & Transcription**
> 
> I. The center of mass of a system moves as if it were a single particle of mass equal to the total mass of the system, acted on by the total external force, and independent of the nature of the internal forces (as long as they follow $f_{\alpha\beta}=-f_{\beta\alpha}$, the weak form of Newton's Third Law).

### Total Linear Momentum

> **Original Notes & Transcription**
> 
> The total linear momentum of the system is
> 
> $$P=\sum_{\alpha}m_{\alpha}\dot{r}_{\alpha}=\frac{d}{dt}\sum_{\alpha}m_{\alpha}r_{\alpha}=\frac{d}{dt}(MR)=M\dot{R}$$
> 
> (9.12) and
> 
> $$\dot{P}=M\ddot{R}=F$$
> 
> (9.13) Thus, the total linear momentum of the system is conserved if there is no external force. From Equations 9.12 and 9.13, we note our second and third important results: II. The linear momentum of the system is the same as if a single particle of mass M were located at the position of the center of mass and moving in the manner the center of mass moves. III. The total linear momentum for a system free of external forces is constant and equal to the linear momentum of the center of mass (the law of conservation of linear momentum for a system). All measurements must be made in an inertial reference system. An example of the linear momentum of a system is given by the explosion of an artillery shell above ground. Because the explosion is an internal effect, the only external force affecting the center of mass velocity is due to gravity. The center of mass of the artillery shell fragments immediately after the explosion must continue with the velocity of the shell just before the explosion.

**Elaboration:**

Equations 9.12 and 9.13 formally establish the **Conservation of Linear Momentum**.

- If $F = 0$ (no net external force), then $\dot{P} = 0$, meaning the momentum $P$ does not change over time.
    
- The artillery shell example is a classic physics illustration. When a shell explodes mid-air, the forces tearing it apart are entirely internal. Therefore, the internal forces sum to zero and cannot change the system's total momentum. If you were to track the parabolic trajectory of the shell's center of mass _before_ it exploded, the center of mass of all the expanding fragments _after_ the explosion will continue to perfectly trace that exact same parabola (ignoring air resistance), governed solely by the external force of gravity.
    

### Example 9.2: The Falling Chain Problem

> **Original Notes & Transcription**
> 
> EXAMPLE 9.2 A chain of uniform linear mass density $\rho$, length $b$, and mass $M$ ($\rho=M/b$) hangs as shown in Figure 9-4. At time $t=0$, the ends A and B are adjacent, but end B is released. Find the tension in the chain at point A after end B has fallen a distance $x$ by (a) assuming free fall and (b) by using energy conservation.

**Elaboration:**

This is an advanced variable-mass problem. The complexity arises because as the chain falls, the mass of the moving portion decreases, while the mass of the stationary portion increases. We are tasked with analyzing it under two different physical assumptions to see how the mathematical models differ.

#### Part (a): Free Fall Assumption

> **Original Notes & Transcription**
> 
> Solution. (a) In the case of free fall, let's assume the only forces acting on the system at time $t$ are the tension $T$ acting vertically upward at point A and the gravitational force $Mg$ pulling the chain down. The center of mass momentum reacts to these forces such that
> 
> $$\dot{P}=Mg-T$$
> 
> (9.14) The right side of the chain, with mass $\rho(b-x)/2$, is moving at the speed $\dot{x}$, and the left side of the chain is not moving. The total momentum of the system is therefore
> 
> $$P=\rho\left(\frac{b-x}{2}\right)\dot{x}$$
> 
> and
> 
> $$\dot{P}=\frac{\rho}{2}[-\dot{x}^{2}+\dot{x}(b-x)]$$
> 
> For free fall, we have $x=gt^{2}/2$ so that
> 
> $$\dot{x}=gt=\sqrt{2gx}$$
> 
> and
> 
> $$\ddot{x}=g$$
> 
> and finally,
> 
> $$\dot{P}=\frac{\rho}{2}(gb-3gx)=Mg-T$$
> 
> (9.15)
> 
> $$T=\frac{Mg}{2}\left(\frac{3x}{b}+1\right)$$
> 
> (9.16)

**Elaboration:**

In this approach, we assume the falling side of the chain drops at exactly the acceleration of gravity ($g$), unhindered by the bend at the bottom.

1. **Defining the Momentum:** Only the falling part of the chain has momentum. The length of the falling part at distance $x$ is $(b-x)/2$, so its mass is $\rho(b-x)/2$. Multiplying by its velocity $\dot{x}$ gives the momentum $P$.
    
2. **Taking the Derivative:** To find $\dot{P}$, we must use the product rule because _both_ the velocity $\dot{x}$ and the mass of the falling portion are changing with time. This yields the $-\dot{x}^2$ term (momentum loss due to mass transferring to the stationary side) and the $\ddot{x}$ term (momentum gain due to acceleration).
    
3. **Solving for Tension:** By substituting the kinematic equations for free fall ($x = \frac{1}{2}gt^2$, etc.) into the $\dot{P}$ equation, we can equate it to the external forces ($Mg - T$) and isolate $T$.
    

#### Part (b): Energy Conservation Assumption

> **Original Notes & Transcription**
> 
> (b) Calkin and March (Am. J. Phys. 57, 154 [1989]) have found that chains act much like a perfectly flexible, inextensible rope that conserves energy when it falls, with no dissipative mechanisms. We treat the chain as one-dimensional motion, ignoring the small horizontal motion. Let the potential energy $U$ be measured relative to the fixed end of the chain, so that the initial potential energy $U(t=0)=U_{0}=-\rho gb^{2}/4$. A careful geometric construction shows that the potential energy after the chain has dropped a distance $x$ is
> 
> $$U=-\frac{1}{4}\rho g(b^{2}+2bx-x^{2})$$
> 
> The kinetic energy (where we use K instead of T to avoid confusion with tension) is determined from the speed $\dot{x}$ of the right side of the chain, so that
> 
> $$K=\frac{\rho}{4}(b-x)\dot{x}^{2}$$
> 
> Because energy is conserved, we must have $K+U=U_{0}$
> 
> $$\frac{\rho}{4}(b-x)\dot{x}^{2}-\frac{1}{4}\rho g(b^{2}+2bx-x^{2})=-\frac{1}{4}\rho gb^{2}$$
> 
> We solve for $\dot{x}^{2}$ to obtain
> 
> $$\dot{x}^{2}=\frac{g(2bx-x^{2})}{b-x}$$
> 
> (9.17) To find the tension from Equations 9.14 and 9.15, we need to determine $\ddot{x}$. We take the derivative of Equation 9.17 and find
> 
> $$\ddot{x}=g+\frac{g(2bx-x^{2})}{2(b-x)^{2}}$$
> 
> We now insert $\dot{x}^{2}$ and $\ddot{x}$ from the two previous equations into Equation 9.15 to determine $\dot{P}$, insert this value of $P$ into Equation 9.14. After collection of terms and solving for T, we obtain
> 
> $$T=\frac{Mg}{4b}\frac{1}{(b-x)}(2b^{2}+2bx-3x^{2})$$
> 
> (9.18)

**Elaboration:**

Here, we reject the free-fall assumption and instead demand that total mechanical energy is conserved.

1. **Setting up Energy:** We calculate the potential energy of the chain by treating it as two distinct hanging segments and finding their respective centers of mass. The kinetic energy is calculated similarly to the momentum above, applying $\frac{1}{2}mv^2$ to the falling portion.
    
2. **Velocity and Acceleration:** Setting $E_{initial} = E_{final}$ allows us to solve algebraically for the squared velocity ($\dot{x}^2$). Differentiating this with respect to time (using the chain rule) yields the acceleration ($\ddot{x}$). Notice that the acceleration is _greater_ than $g$ here! The tension from the bend is actively pulling the falling side down.
    
3. **Calculating Tension:** By substituting these new, energy-conserved values for velocity and acceleration back into the momentum equation ($\dot{P} = Mg - T$), we arrive at a drastically different formula for Tension.
    

> **Original Notes & Transcription**
> 
> Note the difference between the two results, Equations 9.16 and 9.18, for the free fall and energy conserving methods. It should be rather easy by experimentation to determine which is correct, because the latter result has the tension rising dramatically ($T\rightarrow\infty$) at the end when $x\rightarrow b$. Experiments by Calkin and March confirm that the tension does increase rapidly at the end to a maximum of about 25 times the chain's weight, and the observations as a function of $x$ agree well with the calculations. Real chains cannot have an infinite tension. For the free fall case, the tension in the chain is discontinuous on either side of the bottom bend; the tension is $T_{1}=\rho\dot{x}^{2}/2$ on the fixed side and $T_{2}=0$ on the free side. For the energy conserving case, the tension $T_{2}$ on the free side is not zero, and this tension helps gravity pull the chain down. The result is that the chain falls about 15% faster than calculated for the free fall case. For energy-conserving chains, the tension is continuous: $T_{1}=T_{2}=\rho\dot{x}^{2}/4$. We examine further properties of the falling chain in the problems.

**Elaboration:**

This concluding discussion highlights the connection between theoretical mathematical models and experimental physics. While the free-fall assumption is mathematically simpler, the energy conservation model more accurately reflects reality, revealing the counter-intuitive phenomenon that the falling chain actually accelerates _faster_ than gravity. The tension spike at the end (as the denominator $(b-x)$ approaches zero) mathematically shoots to infinity, but in reality, the structural limits of the chain material prevent infinite force, capping it at roughly 25g.
