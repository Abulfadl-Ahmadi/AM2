## 11.9 Euler's Equations for a Rigid Body

> [!abstract] Vault Links
> **Concept Hub:** [[Chapter 11 - Dynamics of Rigid Bodies]] | **Textbook Index:** [[Textbook Ch 11 - Dynamics of Rigid Bodies]] | [[Brain-Map|🧠 Brain Map]]

### Original Transcribed Notes

Let us first consider the force-free motion of a rigid body. In such a case, the potential energy $U$ vanishes, and the Lagrangian $L$ becomes identical with the rotational kinetic energy $T$.[^12] If we choose the body axes to correspond to the principal axes of the body, then from Equation 11.35 we have:

$$T = \frac{1}{2} \left( I_1 \omega_1^2 + I_2 \omega_2^2 + I_3 \omega_3^2 \right)$$
*(11.107)*

If we choose the Eulerian angles as the generalized coordinates, then Lagrange's equation for the $\psi$ coordinate is:

$$\frac{d}{dt} \left( \frac{\partial T}{\partial \dot{\psi}} \right) - \frac{\partial T}{\partial \psi} = 0$$
*(11.108)*

Since $\dot{\psi}$ appears only in the component $\omega_3$ (see Equation 11.102), we have:

$$\frac{\partial T}{\partial \dot{\psi}} = \sum_i \frac{\partial T}{\partial \omega_i} \frac{\partial \omega_i}{\partial \dot{\psi}} = I_3 \omega_3 \frac{\partial \omega_3}{\partial \dot{\psi}} = I_3 \omega_3$$
*(11.109)*

If we differentiate the components of $\boldsymbol{\omega}$ (Equation 11.102) with respect to $\psi$, we have:

$$\begin{aligned}
\frac{\partial \omega_1}{\partial \psi} &= \dot{\phi} \sin \theta \cos \psi - \dot{\theta} \sin \psi = \omega_2 \\
\frac{\partial \omega_2}{\partial \psi} &= -\dot{\phi} \sin \theta \sin \psi - \dot{\theta} \cos \psi = -\omega_1 \\
\frac{\partial \omega_3}{\partial \psi} &= 0
\end{aligned}$$
*(11.110)*

and:

$$\frac{\partial T}{\partial \psi} = \sum_i I_i \omega_i \frac{\partial \omega_i}{\partial \psi} = I_1 \omega_1 \omega_2 - I_2 \omega_2 \omega_1 = (I_1 - I_2) \omega_1 \omega_2$$
*(11.111)*

Equation 11.108 therefore becomes:

$$\frac{d}{dt}(I_3 \omega_3) - (I_1 - I_2) \omega_1 \omega_2 = 0$$
$$I_3 \dot{\omega}_3 - (I_1 - I_2) \omega_1 \omega_2 = 0$$
*(11.113)*

Because the designation of any particular principal axis as the $x_3$-axis is entirely arbitrary, Equation 11.113 can be cyclically permuted to obtain relations for $\dot{\omega}_1$ and $\dot{\omega}_2$:

$$I_1 \dot{\omega}_1 - (I_2 - I_3) \omega_2 \omega_3 = 0$$
$$I_2 \dot{\omega}_2 - (I_3 - I_1) \omega_3 \omega_1 = 0$$
$$I_3 \dot{\omega}_3 - (I_1 - I_2) \omega_1 \omega_2 = 0$$
*(11.114)*

Equations 11.114 are called Euler’s equations for force-free motion.[^13] It must be noted that, although Equation 11.113 for $\dot{\omega}_3$ is indeed the Lagrange equation for the coordinate $\psi$, the Euler equations for $\dot{\omega}_1$ and $\dot{\omega}_2$ are not the Lagrange equations for $\theta$ and $\phi$.

To obtain Euler’s equations for motion in a force field, we may start with the fundamental relation for the torque $\mathbf{N}$:

$$\left( \frac{d\mathbf{L}}{dt} \right)_{\text{fixed}} = \mathbf{N}$$
*(11.115)*

where the designation "fixed" has been explicitly appended to $\mathbf{L}$ because this relation is derived from Newton's equation and is therefore valid only in an inertial frame of reference. From Equation 10.12, we have:

$$\left( \frac{d\mathbf{L}}{dt} \right)_{\text{fixed}} = \left( \frac{d\mathbf{L}}{dt} \right)_{\text{body}} + \boldsymbol{\omega} \times \mathbf{L}$$
*(11.116)*

or:

$$\left( \frac{d\mathbf{L}}{dt} \right)_{\text{body}} + \boldsymbol{\omega} \times \mathbf{L} = \mathbf{N}$$
*(11.117)*

The component of this equation along the $x_3$-axis (note that this is a body axis) is:

$$\dot{L}_3 + (\omega_1 L_2 - \omega_2 L_1) = N_3$$
*(11.118)*

But because we have chosen the $x_i$-axes to coincide with the principal axes of the body, we have $L_i = I_i \omega_i$, so that:

$$I_3 \dot{\omega}_3 - (I_1 - I_2) \omega_1 \omega_2 = N_3$$
*(11.119)*

By permuting the subscripts, we can write all three components of $\mathbf{N}$:

$$I_1 \dot{\omega}_1 - (I_2 - I_3) \omega_2 \omega_3 = N_1$$
$$I_2 \dot{\omega}_2 - (I_3 - I_1) \omega_3 \omega_1 = N_2$$
$$I_3 \dot{\omega}_3 - (I_1 - I_2) \omega_1 \omega_2 = N_3$$
*(11.120)*

Using the permutation symbol $\epsilon_{ijk}$, we can write:

$$I_i \dot{\omega}_i - \sum_{j,k} \epsilon_{ijk} I_j \omega_j \omega_k = N_i$$
*(11.121)*

Equations 11.120 and 11.121 are the desired Euler equations for the motion of a rigid body in a force field.

The motion of a rigid body depends on the structure of the body only through the three numbers $I_1$, $I_2$, and $I_3$—that is, the principal moments of inertia. Thus, any two bodies with the same principal moments move in exactly the same manner, regardless of the fact that they may have quite different shapes. (However, effects such as frictional retardation may depend on the shape of a body.) The simplest geometrical shape that a body having three given principal moments may possess is a homogeneous ellipsoid. The motion of any rigid body can therefore be represented by the motion of the equivalent ellipsoid.[^14] The treatment of rigid-body dynamics from this point of view was originated by Poinsot in 1834. The Poinsot construction is sometimes useful for depicting the motion of a rigid body geometrically.

**EXAMPLE 11.10** Consider the dumbbell of Section 11.4. Find the angular momentum of the system and the torque required to maintain the motion shown in Figures 11-4 and 11-11.

![Figure 11-11](file:///c:/Users/Mester/Desktop/semester%204/AM%20II/exam/textbook/M-Chapter-11/37.png)
**Figure 11-11 Context:** Example 11.10. The dumbbell with masses $m_1$ and $m_2$ at the ends of its shaft has its angular momentum $\mathbf{L}$ perpendicular to the shaft and $\mathbf{L}$ rotates around $\boldsymbol{\omega}$. The shaft maintains an angle $\alpha$ with $\boldsymbol{\omega}$.

**Solution.** Let $|\mathbf{r}_1| = |\mathbf{r}_2| = b$. Let the body-fixed coordinate system have its origin at $O$ (the center of mass) and the symmetry axis $x_3$ be along the weightless shaft toward $m_1$:

$$\mathbf{L} = \sum_{\alpha} m_\alpha \mathbf{r}_\alpha \times \mathbf{v}_\alpha$$
*(11.122)*

Because $\mathbf{L}$ is perpendicular to the shaft and $\mathbf{L}$ rotates around $\boldsymbol{\omega}$ as the shaft rotates, let $\mathbf{e}_2$ be in the plane of the shaft and $\boldsymbol{\omega}$ (with $\mathbf{e}_1$ perpendicular to this plane):

$$\mathbf{L} = L_2 \mathbf{e}_2$$
*(11.123)*

If $\alpha$ is the angle between $\boldsymbol{\omega}$ and the shaft ($x_3$-axis), the components of $\boldsymbol{\omega}$ are:

$$\begin{aligned}
\omega_1 &= 0 \\
\omega_2 &= \omega \sin \alpha \\
\omega_3 &= \omega \cos \alpha
\end{aligned}$$
*(11.124)*

The principal axes are $x_1$, $x_2$, and $x_3$, and the principal moments of inertia are:

$$I_1 = I_2 = (m_1 + m_2)b^2, \quad I_3 = 0$$
*(11.125)*

Combining Equations 11.124 and 11.125:

$$\begin{aligned}
L_1 &= I_1 \omega_1 = 0 \\
L_2 &= I_2 \omega_2 = (m_1 + m_2)b^2 \omega \sin \alpha \\
L_3 &= I_3 \omega_3 = 0
\end{aligned}$$
*(11.126)*

which agrees with Equation 11.123.

Using Euler's equations (Equation 11.120) with $\dot{\omega}_i = 0$ (steady rotation):

$$\begin{aligned}
N_1 &= -(I_2 - I_3) \omega_2 \omega_3 = -(m_1 + m_2)b^2 \omega^2 \sin \alpha \cos \alpha \\
N_2 &= -(I_3 - I_1) \omega_3 \omega_1 = 0 \\
N_3 &= -(I_1 - I_2) \omega_1 \omega_2 = 0
\end{aligned}$$
*(11.127)*

The torque required to maintain the motion if $\dot{\omega} = 0$ is directed along the negative $x_1$-axis.

[^12]: Because the motion is force-free, the translational kinetic energy is unimportant for our purposes here.
[^13]: Leonhard Euler, 1758.
[^14]: The momental ellipsoid was introduced by the French mathematician Baron Augustin-Louis Cauchy (1789–1857) in 1827.
