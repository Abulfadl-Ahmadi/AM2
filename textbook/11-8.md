## 11.8 Eulerian Angles

> [!abstract] Vault Links

### Original Transcribed Notes

The transformation from one coordinate system to another can be represented by a matrix equation of the form:

$$\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{A} \mathbf{x}'$$

If we identify the fixed system with $\mathbf{x}'$ and the body system with $\mathbf{x}$, then the rotation matrix $\mathbf{A}$ completely describes the relative orientation of the two systems. The rotation matrix $\mathbf{A}$ contains three independent angles. There are many possible choices for these angles; we find it convenient to use the Eulerian angles[^10] $\phi$, $\theta$, and $\psi$.

![Figure 11-9](file:///c:/Users/Mester/Desktop/semester%204/AM%20II/exam/textbook/M-Chapter-11/31.png)
**Figure 11-9 Context:** The Eulerian angles are used to rotate from the $x'_i$ system to the $x_i$ system. (a) First rotation is counterclockwise through an angle $\phi$ about the $x'_3$-axis. (b) Second rotation is counterclockwise through an angle $\theta$ about the new $x''_1$-axis (line of nodes). (c) Third rotation is counterclockwise through an angle $\psi$ about the $x'''_3$-axis.

The Eulerian angles are generated in the following series of rotations, which takes the $x'_i$ system into the $x_i$ system:[^11]

1. The first rotation is counterclockwise through an angle $\phi$ about the $x'_3$-axis (Figure 11-9a) to transform the $x'_i$ axes into the $x''_i$ axes. Because the rotation takes place in the $x'_1 - x'_2$ plane, the transformation matrix $\mathbf{A}_\phi$ is:

$$\mathbf{A}_\phi = \begin{pmatrix} \cos \phi & \sin \phi & 0 \\ -\sin \phi & \cos \phi & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$$
*(11.91)*

and:

$$\mathbf{x}'' = \mathbf{A}_\phi \mathbf{x}'$$
*(11.92)*

2. The second rotation is counterclockwise through an angle $\theta$ about the new $x''_1$-axis (the line of nodes, Figure 11-9b) to transform the $x''_i$ axes into the $x'''_i$ axes. Because the rotation is now in the $x''_2 - x''_3$ plane, the transformation matrix $\mathbf{A}_\theta$ is:

$$\mathbf{A}_\theta = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & \cos \theta & \sin \theta \\ 0 & -\sin \theta & \cos \theta \end{pmatrix}$$
*(11.93)*

and:

$$\mathbf{x}''' = \mathbf{A}_\theta \mathbf{x}''$$
*(11.94)*

3. The third rotation is counterclockwise through an angle $\psi$ about the new $x'''_3$-axis (Figure 11-9c) to transform the $x'''_i$ axes into the body $x_i$ axes. The transformation matrix $\mathbf{A}_\psi$ is:

$$\mathbf{A}_\psi = \begin{pmatrix} \cos \psi & \sin \psi & 0 \\ -\sin \psi & \cos \psi & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$$
*(11.95)*

and:

$$\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{A}_\psi \mathbf{x}'''$$
*(11.96)*

The line common to the planes containing the $x'_1 - x'_2$ and $x_1 - x_2$ axes (the intersection of the $x''_1 - x''_2$ and $x'''_1 - x'''_2$ planes) is called the line of nodes. The complete transformation from the $x'_i$ system to the $x_i$ system is given by:

$$\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{A}_\psi \mathbf{x}''' = \mathbf{A}_\psi \mathbf{A}_\theta \mathbf{x}'' = \mathbf{A}_\psi \mathbf{A}_\theta \mathbf{A}_\phi \mathbf{x}'$$
*(11.97)*

and the rotation matrix $\mathbf{A}$ is:

$$\mathbf{A} = \mathbf{A}_\psi \mathbf{A}_\theta \mathbf{A}_\phi$$
*(11.98)*

The components of this matrix are:

$$\begin{aligned}
A_{11} &= \cos\psi\cos\phi - \cos\theta\sin\phi\sin\psi \\
A_{12} &= \cos\psi\sin\phi + \cos\theta\cos\phi\sin\psi \\
A_{13} &= \sin\psi\sin\theta \\
A_{21} &= -\sin\psi\cos\phi - \cos\theta\sin\phi\cos\psi \\
A_{22} &= -\sin\psi\sin\phi + \cos\theta\cos\phi\cos\psi \\
A_{23} &= \cos\psi\sin\theta \\
A_{31} &= \sin\theta\sin\phi \\
A_{32} &= -\sin\theta\cos\phi \\
A_{33} &= \cos\theta
\end{aligned}$$
*(11.99)*

Because we can associate a vector with an infinitesimal rotation, we can associate the time derivatives of these rotation angles with the components of the angular velocity vector $\boldsymbol{\omega}$. Thus,

$$\boldsymbol{\omega}_\phi = \dot{\phi} \mathbf{e}'_3, \quad \boldsymbol{\omega}_\theta = \dot{\theta} \mathbf{e}''_1, \quad \boldsymbol{\omega}_\psi = \dot{\psi} \mathbf{e}_3$$
*(11.100)*

The rigid-body equations of motion are most conveniently expressed in the body coordinate system (i.e., the $x_i$ system), and therefore we must express the components of $\boldsymbol{\omega}$ in this system. We note that the angular velocities $\dot{\phi}$, $\dot{\theta}$, and $\dot{\psi}$ are directed along the following axes:
- $\dot{\phi}$ along the $x'_3$- (fixed) axis
- $\dot{\theta}$ along the line of nodes ($x''_1$-axis)
- $\dot{\psi}$ along the $x_3$- (body) axis

The components of these angular velocities along the body coordinate axes are:

$$\begin{aligned}
\dot{\phi}_1 &= \dot{\phi} \sin \theta \sin \psi \\
\dot{\phi}_2 &= \dot{\phi} \sin \theta \cos \psi \\
\dot{\phi}_3 &= \dot{\phi} \cos \theta
\end{aligned}$$
*(11.101a)*

$$\begin{aligned}
\dot{\theta}_1 &= \dot{\theta} \cos \psi \\
\dot{\theta}_2 &= -\dot{\theta} \sin \psi \\
\dot{\theta}_3 &= 0
\end{aligned}$$
*(11.101b)*

$$\begin{aligned}
\dot{\psi}_1 &= 0 \\
\dot{\psi}_2 &= 0 \\
\dot{\psi}_3 &= \dot{\psi}
\end{aligned}$$
*(11.101c)*

Collecting the individual components of $\boldsymbol{\omega}$, we have, finally:

$$\omega_1 = \dot{\phi}_1 + \dot{\theta}_1 + \dot{\psi}_1 = \dot{\phi} \sin \theta \sin \psi + \dot{\theta} \cos \psi$$
$$\omega_2 = \dot{\phi}_2 + \dot{\theta}_2 + \dot{\psi}_2 = \dot{\phi} \sin \theta \cos \psi - \dot{\theta} \sin \psi$$
$$\omega_3 = \dot{\phi}_3 + \dot{\theta}_3 + \dot{\psi}_3 = \dot{\phi} \cos \theta + \dot{\psi}$$
*(11.102)*

These relations will be of use later in expressing the components of the angular momentum in the body coordinate system.

**EXAMPLE 11.9** Using the Eulerian angles, find the transformation matrix $\mathbf{A}$ that moves the original $x'_3$-axis to the $x_2 - x_3$ plane halfway between $x_2$ and $x_3$ and moves $x'_1$ perpendicular to the $x_2 - x_3$ plane (Figure 11-10).

![Figure 11-10](file:///c:/Users/Mester/Desktop/semester%204/AM%20II/exam/textbook/M-Chapter-11/34.png)
**Figure 11-10 Context:** Example 11.9. We use Eulerian angles to rotate the $x'_i$ system into the $x_i$ system.

**Solution.** The key to transformations using Eulerian angles is the second rotation about the line of nodes, because this single rotation must move $x'_3$ to $x_3$. From the statement of the problem, $x_3$ must be in the $x_2 - x_3$ plane, rotated $45^\circ$ from $x'_3$. The first rotation must move $x'_2$ to $x''_2$ to have the correct position to rotate $x'_3 = x''_3$ to $x'''_3 = x_3$.

In this case, $x''_3 = x'_3$ is rotated $\theta = 45^\circ$ about the line of nodes ($x''_1$-axis) so that $\phi = 0$ and:

$$\mathbf{A}_\phi = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$$
*(11.103)*

$$\mathbf{A}_\theta = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} & \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \\ 0 & -\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} & \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \end{pmatrix}$$
*(11.104)*

The last rotation, $\psi = 90^\circ$, moves the axes to the final desired position:

$$\mathbf{A}_\psi = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 & 0 \\ -1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$$
*(11.105)*

The transformation matrix $\mathbf{A}$ is $\mathbf{A} = \mathbf{A}_\psi \mathbf{A}_\theta \mathbf{A}_\phi = \mathbf{A}_\psi \mathbf{A}_\theta$:

$$\mathbf{A} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 & 0 \\ -1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} & \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \\ 0 & -\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} & \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} & \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \\ -1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & -\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} & \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \end{pmatrix}$$
*(11.106)*

[^10]: The rotation scheme of Euler was first published in 1776.
[^11]: The designations of the Euler angles and even the manner in which they are generated are not universally agreed upon. Therefore, some care must be taken in comparing any results from different sources. The notation used here is that most commonly found in modern texts.
