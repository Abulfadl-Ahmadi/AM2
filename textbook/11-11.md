## 11.11 Motion of a Symmetric Top with One Point Fixed

> [!abstract] Vault Links

### Original Transcribed Notes

Consider a symmetric top with its bottom tip held fixed[^18] rotating in a gravitational field. In our previous development, we have been able to separate the kinetic energy into translational and rotational parts by taking the body's center of mass to be the origin of the rotating or body coordinate system. Alternatively, if we can choose the origins of the fixed and the body coordinate systems to coincide, then the translational kinetic energy vanishes, because $\mathbf{V} = \dot{\mathbf{R}} = 0$. Such a choice is quite convenient for discussing the top, because the stationary tip may then be taken as the origin for both coordinate systems. Figure 11-15 shows the Euler angles for this situation. The $x'_3$- (fixed) axis corresponds to the vertical, and we choose the $x_3$- (body) axis to be the symmetry axis of the top. The distance from the fixed tip to the center of mass is $h$, and the mass of the top is $M$.

![Figure 11-15](file:///c:/Users/Mester/Desktop/semester%204/AM%20II/exam/textbook/M-Chapter-11/44.png)
**Figure 11-15 Context:** A symmetric top with its bottom tip fixed rotates in a gravitational field. The Euler angles relate the $x'_i$ (fixed) axes with the $x_i$ (body) axes. The angle $\psi$ represents the rotation around the $x_3$ symmetry axis.

Because we have a symmetric top, the principal moments of inertia about the $x_1$- and $x_2$-axes are equal: $I_1 = I_2$. We assume $I_3 \neq I_1$. The kinetic energy is then given by:

$$T = \frac{1}{2} \sum_i I_i \omega_i^2 = \frac{1}{2} I_1 \left( \omega_1^2 + \omega_2^2 \right) + \frac{1}{2} I_3 \omega_3^2$$
*(11.149)*

According to Equation 11.102, we have:

$$\omega_1^2 + \omega_2^2 = (\dot{\phi} \sin \theta \sin \psi + \dot{\theta} \cos \psi)^2 + (\dot{\phi} \sin \theta \cos \psi - \dot{\theta} \sin \psi)^2$$
$$\omega_1^2 + \omega_2^2 = \dot{\phi}^2 \sin^2 \theta + \dot{\theta}^2$$
*(11.150a)*

and:

$$\omega_3 = \dot{\phi} \cos \theta + \dot{\psi}$$
*(11.150b)*

Therefore,

$$T = \frac{1}{2} I_1 \left( \dot{\phi}^2 \sin^2 \theta + \dot{\theta}^2 \right) + \frac{1}{2} I_3 \left( \dot{\phi} \cos \theta + \dot{\psi} \right)^2$$
*(11.151)*

Because the potential energy is $U = Mgh \cos \theta$, the Lagrangian becomes:

$$L = T - U = \frac{1}{2} I_1 \left( \dot{\phi}^2 \sin^2 \theta + \dot{\theta}^2 \right) + \frac{1}{2} I_3 \left( \dot{\phi} \cos \theta + \dot{\psi} \right)^2 - Mgh \cos \theta$$
*(11.152)*

The Lagrangian is cyclic in both the $\phi$- and $\psi$-coordinates; the generalized momenta conjugate to these coordinates are therefore constants of the motion:

$$p_\phi = \frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{\phi}} = (I_1 \sin^2 \theta + I_3 \cos^2 \theta)\dot{\phi} + I_3 \dot{\psi} \cos \theta = \text{constant}$$
*(11.153)*

$$p_\psi = \frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{\psi}} = I_3 (\dot{\phi} \cos \theta + \dot{\psi}) = \text{constant}$$
*(11.154)*

Because the cyclic coordinates are angles, the conjugate momenta are angular momenta—the angular momenta along the axes for which $\phi$ and $\psi$ are the rotation angles, that is, the fixed $x'_3$- (or vertical) axis and the body $x_3$- (or symmetry) axis, respectively. We note that this result is physically expected because the gravitational torque is directed along the line of nodes. Hence, the torque can have no component along either the $x'_3$- or the $x_3$-axis, both of which are perpendicular to the line of nodes. Thus, the angular momenta along these axes are constants of the motion.

Equations 11.153 and 11.154 can be solved for $\dot{\phi}$ and $\dot{\psi}$ in terms of $\theta$. From Equation 11.154, we can write:

$$\dot{\psi} = \frac{p_\psi - I_3 \dot{\phi} \cos \theta}{I_3}$$
*(11.155)*

and substituting this result into Equation 11.153, we find:

$$(I_1 \sin^2 \theta + I_3 \cos^2 \theta)\dot{\phi} + (p_\psi - I_3 \dot{\phi} \cos \theta) \cos \theta = p_\phi$$
$$I_1 \dot{\phi} \sin^2 \theta + p_\psi \cos \theta = p_\phi$$

so that:

$$\dot{\phi} = \frac{p_\phi - p_\psi \cos \theta}{I_1 \sin^2 \theta}$$
*(11.156)*

Using this expression for $\dot{\phi}$ in Equation 11.155, we have:

$$\dot{\psi} = \frac{p_\psi}{I_3} - \frac{p_\phi - p_\psi \cos \theta}{I_1 \sin^2 \theta} \cos \theta$$
*(11.157)*

By hypothesis, the system we are considering is conservative; we therefore have the further property that the total energy is a constant of the motion:

$$E = T + U = \frac{1}{2} I_1 \left( \dot{\phi}^2 \sin^2 \theta + \dot{\theta}^2 \right) + \frac{1}{2} I_3 \omega_3^2 + Mgh \cos \theta = \text{constant}$$
*(11.158)*

Using the expression for $\omega_3$ (Equation 11.150b), we note that Equation 11.154 can be written as:

$$I_3 \omega_3 = p_\psi = \text{constant}$$
*(11.159a)*

so that:

$$\omega_3 = \frac{p_\psi}{I_3} = \text{constant}$$
*(11.159b)*

Therefore, not only is $E$ a constant of the motion, but so is the term $\frac{1}{2} I_3 \omega_3^2$. If we subtract this constant term from $E$, we obtain the modified energy $E'$, which is also a constant of the motion:

$$E' \equiv E - \frac{p_\psi^2}{2 I_3} = \frac{1}{2} I_1 \left( \dot{\phi}^2 \sin^2 \theta + \dot{\theta}^2 \right) + Mgh \cos \theta = \text{constant}$$
*(11.160)*

Substituting into this equation the expression for $\dot{\phi}$ (Equation 11.156), we have:

$$E' = \frac{1}{2} I_1 \dot{\theta}^2 + \frac{(p_\phi - p_\psi \cos \theta)^2}{2 I_1 \sin^2 \theta} + Mgh \cos \theta$$
*(11.161)*

which we can write as:

$$\frac{1}{2} I_1 \dot{\theta}^2 + V(\theta) = E'$$
*(11.162)*

where $V(\theta)$ is an "effective potential" given by:

$$V(\theta) = \frac{(p_\phi - p_\psi \cos \theta)^2}{2 I_1 \sin^2 \theta} + Mgh \cos \theta$$
*(11.163)*

Equation 11.162 can be solved to yield $t(\theta)$:

$$t(\theta) = \int \frac{\sqrt{I_1 / 2}\,d\theta}{\sqrt{E' - V(\theta)}}$$
*(11.164)*

This integral can (formally, at least) be inverted to obtain $\theta(t)$, which, in turn, can be substituted into Equations 11.156 and 11.157 to yield $\phi(t)$ and $\psi(t)$. Because the Euler angles $\theta$, $\phi$, and $\psi$ completely specify the orientation of the top, the results for $\theta(t)$, $\phi(t)$, and $\psi(t)$ constitute a complete solution for the problem. It should be clear that such a procedure is complicated and not very illuminating. But we can obtain some qualitative features of the motion by examining the preceding equations in a manner analogous to that used for treating the motion of a particle in a central-force field (see Section 8.6).

Figure 11-16 shows the form of the effective potential $V(\theta)$ in the range $0 \le \theta \le \pi$, which is the physically limited region for $\theta$. This energy diagram indicates that for any general values of $E'$ (e.g., the value represented by $E'_1$), the motion is limited by two extreme values of $\theta$—that is, $\theta_1$ and $\theta_2$, which correspond to the turning points of the central-force problem and are roots of the denominator in Equation 11.164.

![Figure 11-16](file:///c:/Users/Mester/Desktop/semester%204/AM%20II/exam/textbook/M-Chapter-11/47.png)
**Figure 11-16 Context:** The effective potential $V(\theta)$ for the rotating top of Figure 11-15 is plotted versus the angle $\theta$. We can study the angular limits of the inclination of the top by knowing the modified energy $E'$.

Thus we find that the inclination of the rotating top is, in general, confined to the region $\theta_1 \le \theta \le \theta_2$. For the case that $E' = E'_0 = V_{\text{min}}$, $\theta$ is limited to the single value $\theta_0$, and the motion is a steady precession at a fixed angle of inclination. Such motion is similar to the occurrence of circular orbits in the central-force problem.

The value of $\theta_0$ can be obtained by setting the derivative of $V(\theta)$ equal to zero:

$$\frac{\partial V}{\partial \theta} \Big|_{\theta_0} = -\frac{\cos \theta_0 (p_\phi - p_\psi \cos \theta_0)^2}{I_1 \sin^3 \theta_0} + \frac{p_\psi (p_\phi - p_\psi \cos \theta_0)}{I_1 \sin \theta_0} - Mgh \sin \theta_0 = 0$$
*(11.165)*

If we define the precessional angular velocity as $\dot{\phi}_0 \equiv \frac{p_\phi - p_\psi \cos \theta_0}{I_1 \sin^2 \theta_0}$, then Equation 11.165 becomes:

$$I_1 \cos \theta_0 \dot{\phi}_0^2 - I_3 \omega_3 \dot{\phi}_0 + Mgh = 0$$
*(11.167)*

This is a quadratic in $\dot{\phi}_0$ and can be solved with the result:

$$\dot{\phi}_0 = \frac{I_3 \omega_3 \pm \sqrt{I_3^2 \omega_3^2 - 4 I_1 Mgh \cos \theta_0}}{2 I_1 \cos \theta_0}$$
*(11.168)*

Because $\dot{\phi}_0$ must be a real quantity, the radicand in Equation 11.168 must be positive. If $\theta_0 < \pi/2$, we must have:

$$I_3^2 \omega_3^2 \ge 4 I_1 Mgh \cos \theta_0$$
*(11.169)*

or:

$$\omega_3 \ge \frac{2}{I_3}\sqrt{I_1 Mgh \cos \theta_0}$$
*(11.170)*

We therefore conclude that a steady precession can occur at the fixed angle of inclination $\theta_0$ only if the angular velocity of spin is larger than the limiting value given by Equation 11.170.

We therefore have two possible values of the precessional angular velocity $\dot{\phi}_0$, one for each of the values given by Equation 11.168:
- $\dot{\phi}_0^{(+)}$ $\to$ Fast precession
- $\dot{\phi}_0^{(-)}$ $\to$ Slow precession

If $\omega_3$ (or $p_\psi$) is large (a fast top), then the second term in the radicand of Equation 11.168 is small, and we may expand the radical. Retaining only the first nonvanishing term in each case, we find:

$$\dot{\phi}_0^{(+)} \approx \frac{I_3 \omega_3}{I_1 \cos \theta_0}$$
*(11.172)*

$$\dot{\phi}_0^{(-)} \approx \frac{Mgh}{I_3 \omega_3}$$
*(11.173)*

It is the slower of the two possible precessional angular velocities, $\dot{\phi}_0^{(-)}$, that is usually observed.

The preceding results apply if $\theta_0 < \pi/2$; but if $\theta_0 > \pi/2$,[^19] the radicand in Equation 11.168 is always positive, and there is no limiting condition on $\omega_3$. Because the radical is greater than unity in such a case, the values of $\dot{\phi}_0$ for fast and slow precession have opposite signs; that is, for $\theta_0 > \pi/2$, the fast precession is in the same direction as that for $\theta_0 < \pi/2$, but the slow precession takes place in the opposite sense.

For the general case, in which $\theta_1 \le \theta \le \theta_2$, Equation 11.156 indicates that $\dot{\phi}$ may or may not change sign as $\theta$ varies between its limits—depending on the values of $p_\phi$ and $p_\psi$. If $\dot{\phi}$ does not change sign, the top precesses monotonically around the fixed $x'_3$-axis, and the symmetry axis oscillates between $\theta = \theta_1$ and $\theta = \theta_2$. This phenomenon is called nutation; the path described by the projection of the body symmetry axis on a unit sphere in the fixed system is shown in Figure 11-17a.

If $\dot{\phi}$ does change sign between the limiting values of $\theta$, the precessional angular velocity must have opposite signs at $\theta = \theta_1$ and $\theta = \theta_2$. Thus, the nutational-precessional motion produces the looping motion of the symmetry axis depicted in Figure 11-17b.

![Figure 11-17](file:///c:/Users/Mester/Desktop/semester%204/AM%20II/exam/textbook/M-Chapter-11/49.png)
**Figure 11-17 Context:** The rotating top also nutates between the limit angles $\theta_1$ and $\theta_2$. In (a) $\dot{\phi}$ does not change sign. In (b) $\dot{\phi}$ does change sign, and we see looping motion. In (c) the initial conditions include $\dot{\theta} = 0$ at $\theta = \theta_1$; this is the normal cusp-like motion when we spin a top and release it.

Finally, if the values of $p_\phi$ and $p_\psi$ are such that:

$$p_\phi - p_\psi \cos \theta_1 = 0$$
*(11.174)*

then:

$$\dot{\phi} \Big|_{\theta = \theta_1} = 0$$
*(11.175)*

Figure 11-17c shows the resulting cusplike motion. It is just this case that corresponds to the usual method of starting a top. First, the top is spun around its axis, then it is given a certain initial tilt and released. Thus, initial conditions are $\theta = \theta_1$ and $\dot{\theta} = 0 = \dot{\phi}$. Because the first motion of the top is to begin to fall in the gravitational field, the conditions are exactly those of Figure 11-17c, and the cusplike motion ensues. Figures 11-17a and 11-17b correspond to the motion in the event that there is an initial angular velocity $\dot{\phi}$ either in the direction of or opposite to the direction of precession.

[^18]: This problem was first solved in detail by Lagrange in *Mécanique analytique*.
[^19]: If $\theta_0 > \pi/2$, the fixed tip of the top is at a position above the center of mass. Such motion is possible, for example, with a gyroscopic top whose tip is actually a ball and rests in a cup that is fixed atop a pedestal.
